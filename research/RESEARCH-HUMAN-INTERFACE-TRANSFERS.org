* Preface
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: preface
:END:

This document continues a research question raised by the previous
50-iteration loop on literate programming. That loop's Obs 9 made
a sharp observation: /Nielsen Norman Group's 1997–2017 web-reader
research transfers to AI agent readers with almost zero
adaptation/. The mechanism was shared /evaluation primitives/
(search-arrival, time-bounded evaluation, scan-first-read-later),
not shared cognition. The two populations have different minds
but the same interface.

If that finding generalises — and there is no obvious reason it
should not — then a thick body of human-interface (HI) research
accumulated over fifty years should be partly portable into
LP-with-AI and writer-craft tooling. This document maps which
parts move, which do not, and what the boundary looks like.

** Scope
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: scope
:END:

The research is organised around /ten HI directions/ A through J,
each surveyed across five rounds of progressive depth. For every
direction the body answers six questions:

1. /Specific claim/ of the HI finding (named principle + canonical
   wording)
2. /Empirical basis/ (study, N, method, year)
3. Does the /AI agent show isomorphic behaviour/? Evidence from
   agent traces, system prompts, observed practice?
4. If yes — what is the /shared primitive/ (analogous to the
   search-arrival + time-bounded + scan-first triad of Obs 9)?
5. If no — where does the /transfer break/? Substrate mismatch?
   Attention vs production mismatch?
6. /Operational takeaway/ for the LP author or working writer.

* A. Information foraging
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A
:END:

Information foraging theory is the cleanest pre-existing
match for the seed observation, and anchors the upper-left
full-transfer quadrant of the figure. Developed by Peter
Pirolli and Stuart Card at Xerox PARC from CHI 1995 through
the 1999 =Psychological Review= formulation and the 2007
book, the theory borrows /optimal foraging theory/ from
behavioural ecology — animals making rate-of-gain decisions
about which patches to exploit and when to leave — and
applies the same mathematics to humans searching for
information. The central concept of /information scent/ —
/"the imperfect perception of the value, cost, or access
path of information sources obtained from proximal cues"/ —
predicts the continue-vs-leave behaviour of human web
readers, and the agent grep / read-file / continue-vs-stop
loop exhibits the same patch-leaving shape under the same
cost-benefit structure.

** A.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-1
:END:

Pirolli and Card's central claim is that human information-
seeking is /rate-of-gain optimisation/ over time. The
foundational equation is R = G/T, where R is the rate of
information gain, G is the cumulative benefit (relevant
information acquired), and T is the time invested. Within an
information patch (a collection of related items — a search-
results page, a long document, a directory of files), the
forager gains information quickly at first and slowly later;
the optimal strategy, per Charnov's 1976 marginal-value
theorem from behavioural ecology, is to leave the patch when
its instantaneous within-patch rate drops to the average
between-patch rate (start-up cost + time spent foraging).
The /scent of information/ is the parallel claim about
patch /selection/: proximal cues (link text, file name,
section heading, snippet) carry imperfect information about
the distal payoff (the full content behind the cue). Strong
scent supports good patch choice; weak or misleading scent
produces unproductive foraging.

** A.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-2
:END:

The empirical programme spans roughly fifteen years. Pirolli
and Card's CHI 1995 paper "Information Foraging in
Information Access Environments" (=Proc. CHI '95=, ACM Press,
pp. 51–58; [[https://doi.org/10.1145/223904.223911][DOI 10.1145/223904.223911]]) introduced the
theory with data from an early UNIX file-system access study.
The 1999 =Psychological Review= article (106(4), pp.
643–675; [[https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643][DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.106.4.643]]) presented the
formal model together with eye-tracking and behavioural-log
data from web-search tasks. The 2007 Oxford UP book
=Information Foraging Theory: Adaptive Interaction with
Information= consolidated the empirical work and added
/ACT-IF/ (an integration with the ACT-R cognitive
architecture) and /SNIF-ACT/, the latter formalised in Fu &
Pirolli's "SNIF-ACT: A Cognitive Model of User Navigation
on the World Wide Web" =Human–Computer Interaction= 22(4),
2007, pp. 355–412 ([[https://doi.org/10.1080/07370020701638806][DOI 10.1080/07370020701638806]]). Olston
and Chi 2003 ("ScentTrails: Integrating Browsing and
Searching on the Web," =ACM TOCHI= 10(3), pp. 177–197;
[[https://doi.org/10.1145/937549.937550][DOI 10.1145/937549.937550]]) implemented and evaluated the
=ScentTrails= interface, demonstrating that explicit
visualisation of scent gradients shortened task-completion
times on hard search tasks. The recurring finding across
studies: scent strength predicts patch-selection accuracy
better than other variables including patch content quality.

** A.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-3
:END:

The isomorphism is strong and observable. An agent grepping a
codebase decides /which file to Read next/ from a list of
candidates — exactly a patch-selection problem. The cues
available are filenames, directory paths, grep-context
snippets, function signatures, and (if previously seen)
prose preambles. The agent consumes tokens (the agent's "time"
budget) on each Read; when token cost outpaces information
gain (the file turned out to be unrelated or already-known),
the agent leaves the patch and moves to another candidate.
This pattern matches Pirolli-Card's continue-vs-leave
decision and Charnov's marginal-value theorem in structural
form. The /failure mode/ also rhymes: when proximal cues
mislead — a file named =auth.py= that actually contains only
test helpers, a section heading that promises content the
section never delivers — the agent (like a human) wastes
budget on bad patches. The =CLAUDE.md= "Tool-Use Protocol"
section of the host project, which mandates "every claim
backed by tool output," is an /anti-misleading-scent rule/
written for agents.

** A.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-4
:END:

The shared primitive is the three-part structure: /bounded
budget/ + /unequal patches/ + /asymmetric proximal-distal
information cost/. Bounded budget — humans have attention-
time, agents have tokens; both finite. Unequal patches — the
information landscape is not uniform; some sources pay off
better than others. Asymmetric cost — the cheap proximal cue
carries imperfect information about the expensive distal
payoff. When these three constraints hold, rate-of-gain
optimisation and marginal-value-theorem patch-leaving emerge
by mathematical necessity, regardless of substrate. Neither
human cognition nor transformer attention is required to
generate the behaviour; the constraint structure does.

** A.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-5
:END:

The transfer is not perfect at two boundaries. First, the
/cost currency/ differs — humans pay attention-time
(cognitively expensive, subject to fatigue and to the
working-memory constraints of direction C), agents pay
tokens (uniformly priced, no fatigue, but absolute context-
window bounds). An agent therefore sustains longer foraging
runs within a single session than a human can in one
sitting, but cannot continue across sessions without
retrieval augmentation. Second, the /scent-encoding
substrate/ differs — humans pick up scent through visual
parsing of typography, layout, and short-form text; agents
pick it up through token-level processing of file paths,
header structure, and prose. The structural-vs-typographic
split that follows is articulated in
[[#dir-J-6][J.6 operational takeaway]].

** A.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-6
:END:

Three practices follow directly from information-foraging
theory for the LP author. First, /strengthen proximal cues at
the entry points/: file names that name the concept (not the
mechanism), README first paragraphs that state the value
proposition, section headings that name the role rather than
the phase (the heading-as-concept rule already enforced in
the host project's =.claude/rules/=). Second, /reduce
misleading scent/: a section heading that promises content
the body does not deliver wastes both human and agent budget.
Third, /minimise patch-leave friction at the points where
foraging continues/: cross-references (=CUSTOM_ID= anchors),
"see also" links, and named cross-block references all reduce
the cost of moving from one patch to another, which the
marginal-value theorem rewards directly. None of these
practices requires separating human-target from agent-target
optimisation; the same structural moves serve both audiences.

** A.7 Synthesis: A as full-transfer figure anchor
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-7
:END:

Direction A occupies the /upper-left/ cell of the
transfer-gradient figure — full transfer,
behaviour-and-decision-structure shared with near-zero
adaptation. The reason A transfers cleanly is that
Pirolli-Card's mathematics (Charnov's marginal-value theorem,
R = G/T optimisation, scent-driven patch selection) is
substrate-independent: it derives from constraint structure
(bounded budget + unequal patches + asymmetric proximal-
distal information cost), not from the specifics of human
cognition.

A is the per-patch member of the /Foraging-economics/
family ([[#synthesis-families][synthesis-families]]) — A handles within-patch
rate-of-gain decisions, [[#dir-F][F]] supplies the recognition
asymmetry that makes scent-driven cues cheap, [[#dir-I][I]]
handles iteration across patches. Each direction describes
a /behaviour shape/ (continue-vs-leave, recognise-vs-recall,
iterate-vs-formulate-once) rather than a substrate
mechanism; the family transfers because the architecture
is substrate-independent.

A's specific contribution is the closed-form mathematical
core. Once R = G/T is accepted as substrate-independent,
the whole foraging vocabulary (scent, patch, diminishing
returns, marginal-value-theorem patch-leaving, proximal
cue, distal payoff) ports into the AI-agent setting with
names intact. The NN/g seed observation that motivated the
broader loop is a /specialisation/ of A: web-reader
scannability is patch-selection on the search-engine
results page; information scent is the shared primitive.

* B. Cognitive load theory
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B
:END:

John Sweller introduced /cognitive load theory/ (CLT) in his
1988 paper "Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on
Learning" (=Cognitive Science= 12(2), pp. 257–285;
[[https://doi.org/10.1207/s15516709cog1202_4][DOI 10.1207/s15516709cog1202_4]]), formalising the observation
that human short-term memory is severely bounded and that any
task requiring many items in working memory may exceed that
budget. The theory's /mature/ form, articulated a decade
later in Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas 1998 (=Educational
Psychology Review= 10(3), pp. 251–296), distinguishes three
sources of load: /intrinsic/ from the inherent complexity of
the material (element interactivity), /extraneous/ from poor
presentation choices that do not contribute to learning, and
/germane/ from the effortful schema-construction that does.
Jeroen van Merriënboer extended the framework into the 4C/ID
instructional-design model (=Training Complex Cognitive
Skills=, 1997). CLT sits at /middle-left/ of the
transfer-gradient figure: intrinsic and extraneous load
transfer to AI agents with the substrate (context window +
attention) re-parameterised, but germane load — the
schema-construction educational payoff — breaks at the
agent's stateless cold-start.

** B.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-1
:END:

Sweller's central claim is that working memory has a fixed,
small capacity and that /learning succeeds only when the
total load on that capacity stays under threshold/. Total
load decomposes into three sources: /intrinsic/ load from
the material's inherent complexity (Sweller's operational
measure is /element interactivity/ — the count of items that
must be held simultaneously to understand one new fact),
/extraneous/ load from presentation choices that do not
contribute to schema construction (poor layout, redundant
information, split attention across sources), and /germane/
load from the effortful work of constructing new schemas
which, once built, bypass working-memory limits entirely.
The design imperative that follows: minimise extraneous load,
manage intrinsic load via sequencing, leave room for germane
load.

** B.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-2
:END:

The empirical foundation is broad and replicated. Sweller's
1988 =Cognitive Science= paper presented the seminal data:
students solving algebra problems via means-end analysis
performed worse on subsequent transfer tests than students
who studied worked examples — because means-end analysis
exhausts working memory on goal-tracking, leaving nothing
for schema construction. Sweller & Cooper 1985 in =Cognition
and Instruction= 2(1) established the /worked-example effect/
across mathematics and physics learning. Chandler & Sweller
1992 in =British Journal of Educational Psychology= 62(2)
demonstrated the /split-attention effect/ — when text and
diagram must be integrated to make sense, separating them
spatially imposes extraneous load. Sweller, van Merriënboer,
and Paas (1998) consolidated the /element-interactivity
effect/ — high-interactivity tasks (algebra, programming)
benefit more from extraneous-load reduction than
low-interactivity tasks (vocabulary memorisation). The
mature framework appears in van Merriënboer's 1997 book
=Training Complex Cognitive Skills= and the 2017 third
edition of =Ten Steps to Complex Learning=.

** B.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-3
:END:

The isomorphism is partial. Agents do not show a hard 4±1
cliff (that is direction C's substrate-bound finding) but
they do show /load-dependent degradation/: comprehension and
accuracy drop as the count of simultaneously-interacting
items in the context rises. Element-interactivity transfers
directly — when an agent must refactor across seven mutually
referencing modules, it performs worse than when refactoring
seven modules with no mutual references. Extraneous load
transfers too — cluttered formatting, inconsistent naming,
and redundant information visibly degrade agent output
quality (the host project's =.claude/rules/= files exist
specifically to reduce this kind of extraneous load).
Germane load is where the analogy strains: humans build
durable schemas through deliberate practice that becomes
permanent. Agents within a single inference do not build
durable schemas; chain-of-thought reasoning produces
within-session structure that vanishes at the next prompt.

** B.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-4
:END:

The shared primitive is /a bounded attention budget across
heterogeneous items, with graceful degradation under
overload/. The substrate differs sharply — biological
working memory is a roughly four-slot store with discrete
chunk replacement; transformer attention is a continuous
weight distribution over thousands of tokens with positional
bias. But the structural constraint is genuinely shared:
both have a finite budget, both lose accuracy when budget
is exceeded, and both reward design choices that reduce
extraneous load. The intrinsic / extraneous / germane
three-way split also rhymes — intrinsic load is the
inherent complexity of the task (refactoring multi-module
code is hard for both populations), extraneous load is the
penalty for poor presentation (cluttered prose hurts both),
and germane load is the work of building durable
understanding (where the analogy weakens).

** B.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-5
:END:

The break is at the /germane-load/ construct. CLT's central
educational claim is that good instruction maximises germane
load — the effortful work of building schemas that will
later bypass working memory entirely. This depends on
/durable consolidation/ in long-term memory, which humans
do across sessions and which LLMs do not do during
inference. An agent that "learns" to refactor by being shown
many worked examples in a single context window has no
mechanism to convert that exposure into a durable schema
queryable in the next session; the next session starts fresh.
Fine-tuning is the only architectural analogue, and it is
out of band — not something the agent does as part of normal
interaction. This is the cleanest substrate-mismatch break
in the direction: the structural form (load decomposition,
extraneous-load reduction) transfers, but the educational
mechanism that gives CLT its name does not.

** B.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-6
:END:

Three practices follow from CLT for the LP author, each
serving both audiences. First, /reduce extraneous load/:
consistent =.org= formatting, no cluttered cross-references,
no redundant prose echoing the code block immediately below.
The host project's =lp-prose-no-self-narration.md= rule is a
direct CLT-compatible move. Second, /respect element
interactivity/ when sequencing concepts: introduce one new
interacting mechanism per section, not several. If a module
must explain three mutually-dependent abstractions, give
each its own sub-section with the dependencies stated
explicitly rather than crammed into one paragraph. Third,
/do not expect durable schemas to form from a single
exposure/ — neither human nor agent. Cross-references and
revisits are necessary; chain-of-thought scaffolding within
a section helps the agent's within-session reasoning the way
worked examples help human learning. CLT also predicts the
specific failure mode of dense LP docs that try to do too
much: extraneous-load overflow looks like the agent
"missing the point" or the human reader "getting lost."

** B.7 Synthesis: B in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-7
:END:

Direction B sits at /middle-left of the partial-transfer
diagonal/ in [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]] — a /two-out-of-three/
transfer. Intrinsic and extraneous load decomposition ports
without modification; germane load (the schema-construction
educational payoff) breaks at the agent substrate.

B instantiates two shared-primitive families ([[#synthesis-families][synthesis-
families]]) simultaneously:

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where B sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-C][C. Working memory limits]] | /Element-interactivity bound/ | B and C share the structural form; B's bound is "cognitive load," C's bound is "chunk count" — same primitive, two literatures |
| [[#dir-E][E. Mental models]], [[#dir-G][G. Progressive disclosure]], [[#dir-I][I. Berry-picking]] | /Cross-session state persistence/ | B's germane load is the schema-construction member; same break boundary as E's mental-model accretion, G's expert-recognition payoff, I's saturated-sampling memory |

CLT is the cleanest example of cross-session state
persistence breaking transfer: the theory explicitly names
germane load as the educational payoff, and the educational
mechanism that gives the theory its name does not transfer.
E, G and I break at the same boundary for different reasons
but share the primitive: /the finding requires the reader to
carry state from one encounter to the next/. Agent
architecture (cold-start each conversation) violates this
precondition.

The behaviour-vs-substrate axis explains B's position: CLT's
structural form (decomposition, bounded budget, extraneous
reduction) lives at the behaviour layer and transfers; its
educational form (germane load builds durable schemas) lives
at the substrate layer and does not. Two halves of one
theory project onto opposite cells of the figure.

* C. Working memory limits
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C
:END:

Direction C is the substrate-level finding that makes
direction B (cognitive load theory) meaningful in the first
place: human short-term storage is /numerically bounded/, and
the bound is small. George Miller's 1956 paper "The Magical
Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two" (=Psychological Review=
63(2), pp. 81–97) reviewed studies on absolute judgement and
memory span and proposed roughly seven items. Forty-five
years later Nelson Cowan's "The Magical Number 4 in
Short-Term Memory" (=Behavioral and Brain Sciences= 24(1),
2001, pp. 87–114, plus extensive peer commentary to p. 185)
revised the estimate downward — once chunking and rehearsal
are controlled out, the focus of attention holds roughly four
items, three to five at the band edges. Alan Baddeley's multi-component working-memory model (1974
onward, mature form in =Working Memory=, 1986) decomposes
the store into phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad,
episodic buffer, and a central executive. C sits at
/partial-low/ of the figure: the structural shape (capacity
is bounded; chunking mitigates) transfers to AI agents, but
the numerical constants (Miller's 7, Cowan's 4) do not —
agent context windows degrade gradiently across thousands
of tokens, not at a sharp four-item cliff.

** C.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-1
:END:

Three claims sit nested inside this direction. Miller's
1956 paper ([[https://doi.org/10.1037/h0043158][DOI 10.1037/h0043158]]) proposed roughly
/seven plus or minus two/ chunks for human short-term
storage, derived from /absolute-judgement/ tasks (categorising
stimuli along one dimension) and /immediate-memory/
experiments (digit, letter, word recall) — Miller's paper
explicitly distinguishes the two task families, a distinction
later popular writing often elides. Miller himself
characterised the number as a "rhetorical device" rather than
a precise cognitive limit; the empirical signal he observed
was real but the formula was a marketing line. Cowan's 2001
reconsideration ([[https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X01003922][DOI 10.1017/S0140525X01003922]]) controlled
out chunking, rehearsal, and long-term-memory retrieval,
isolating the /focus of attention/ at /four chunks plus or
minus one/, with three to five at the band edges across
modalities and task types. Baddeley's multi-component model adds structural
detail: the bounded store decomposes into a /phonological
loop/ (verbal-acoustic items, rehearsed via sub-vocal
speech), a /visuospatial sketchpad/ (visual-spatial items),
an /episodic buffer/ (cross-modal binding), and a /central
executive/ that gates entry and allocates attention.

** C.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-2
:END:

The empirical record spans seven decades. Miller 1956
synthesised absolute-judgement studies (subjects categorising
tones along a single dimension) and memory-span experiments
(digits, letters, words), finding consistent ceilings in the
five-to-nine range. Cowan's 2001 =Behavioral and Brain
Sciences= target article (with 25 invited commentaries
running to p. 185) reviewed verbal, non-verbal, visual,
auditory, single-task, dual-task, attended, and unattended
studies — all converging on three-to-five chunks once
chunking and rehearsal were controlled. The robustness of
the lower bound across modalities is the result's strongest
feature. Baddeley & Hitch 1974 in =The Psychology of
Learning and Motivation= introduced the multi-component
model; Baddeley 1986 book gave it textbook form;
Baddeley 2000 added the episodic buffer; the 2012 =Annual
Review of Psychology= article presented the mature
consolidation including dual-task interference patterns that
isolate sub-component capacity. Recent neuroimaging work
(Pessoa et al. 2002; Todd & Marois 2004 =Nature= 428,
pp. 751–754) located the four-item bound to specific
parietal-cortex activation patterns, giving the cognitive
finding a neural substrate.

** C.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-3
:END:

The numerical limits do not transfer. Agents have context
windows measured in thousands or hundreds of thousands of
tokens, not a hard four-item cliff. Degradation under
overload is /gradient/ — accuracy declines as context fills
toward the limit, with positional bias (Liu et al. 2024
"Lost in the Middle" finding showed worse retrieval from
middle portions of long contexts) and attention dilution
rather than a discrete capacity wall. However, the
/functional behaviour/ does rhyme. When an agent must
simultaneously hold many interacting facts in working
context — refactoring code across multiple modules, tracing
a bug through nested function calls, answering a question
requiring synthesis of facts scattered across several files
— accuracy degrades in a load-dependent way that resembles
the human pattern at a much larger scale. The /chunking/
mitigation transfers cleanly: agents perform better on a
ten-file refactor when each file is named and summarised
upfront (chunks made explicit) than when they have to
discover the structure during the task.

** C.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-4
:END:

The shared primitive is the /structural form/ of the
constraint, not the numerical bound: raw simultaneous
capacity is too small for non-trivial tasks, and /chunking/
restores capability by treating composite items as single
units. Humans chunk by retrieving consolidated schemas from
long-term memory (the chess-master example: a board
position that looks like sixteen pieces to a novice
collapses into three or four familiar patterns for a
grandmaster). Agents chunk via retrieval-augmented
generation (load a summary instead of the raw text),
named-block references (=#+NAME:= + =:noweb yes=), and
explicit named-concept anchoring (=CUSTOM_ID= cross-references).
The mathematical primitive is identical — turn many items
into few units by giving the units names — even though the
encoding mechanism differs entirely.

** C.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-5
:END:

Three break points are visible. First, /the numerical limits/
(4, 7, etc.) have no obvious agent analogue and no
operational meaning — there is no point in instructing the
LP author to "keep simultaneous concepts under four" when
the agent's effective limit is hundreds or thousands. The
specific bound is human-biology specific. Second,
/Baddeley's multi-component decomposition/ — phonological
loop, visuospatial sketchpad, episodic buffer — maps poorly
onto transformer architectures which have no modality-
specific buffers and no central executive separate from the
content stream. Third, /the Cowan vs Miller debate itself/
is not a debate the agent has — both the seven and four
estimates come from specific biological experimental
protocols, none of which the agent participated in. The
debate is /illuminating/ when read by a transformer
architect (what is /our/ "magical number"?) but it is not
literally the agent's debate.

** C.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-6
:END:

Three practices follow from the working-memory literature
for the LP author. First, /limit simultaneously-interacting
concepts per section/. For human readers the empirical
target is roughly four; for agent readers the target is
"well under context budget" but the qualitative move is the
same — break a heavy section into sub-sections with
explicit chunking. Second, /use named chunks/: =#+NAME:=
blocks, =CUSTOM_ID= cross-references, and consistent
heading-as-concept rules. The host project's
=naming-conventions.md= rule does this for code; the same
principle applies to prose preambles. Third, /do not chase
the specific number/. The seven and four estimates are
human-biology specific; what transfers is the /shape/ —
raw capacity is too small for non-trivial tasks, and the
mitigation is chunking. Both human and agent readers benefit
from explicit chunking infrastructure (named anchors,
cross-references, summaries) even though the implementation
of "too small" differs by orders of magnitude.

** C.7 Synthesis: C in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-7
:END:

Direction C sits at /partial-low on the partial-transfer
diagonal/ in [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]] — the qualitative shape ("raw
capacity is small; chunking mitigates") transfers, the
specific numerical constants do not. C is the cleanest
example of the substrate-bound-numerical-constants pattern
and anchors the eponymous shared-primitive family.

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where C sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-B][B. Cognitive load theory]] | /Element-interactivity bound/ | C is the working-memory-literature side of the same constraint B states from the cognitive-load side |
| [[#dir-D][D. Eye-tracking + saccades]], [[#dir-H][H. Gestalt principles]] | /Substrate-bound numerical constants/ | C contributes Miller's 7±2 and Cowan's 4±1; D contributes Rayner's saccade lengths and parafoveal-span pixel ranges; H contributes Wertheimer / Kubovy-van den Berg grouping radii |

Three properties distinguish the /Substrate-bound numerical
constants/ family from other partial-transfer directions:

1. /A specific number is part of the canonical finding./
   Miller's title is literally "The Magical Number Seven";
   Cowan's reconsideration is titled "The Magical Number 4";
   Rayner reports saccade lengths in degrees of visual angle;
   Wertheimer reports grouping thresholds in millimetres.
2. /The number derives from biological apparatus./ Working-
   memory bounds come from neural / phonological-loop
   measurements; saccade lengths from oculomotor and foveal
   geometry; grouping radii from retinal sampling rates.
3. /The structural form remains useful after the number is
   stripped./ "Capacity is bounded" / "perception jumps in
   discrete units" / "proximity produces grouping" port to
   agent design as qualitative rules without their numbers.

The figure's lower-left quadrant therefore contains the
substrate-bound numerical-constant directions in a clean
stripe: C partial-low (numbers are wrong but structure
holds), D human-only (saccade research has no agent
analogue at all), H human-only (Gestalt grouping is
perceptual). C transfers more than D and H because
"capacity is bounded" is a behaviour-level claim that
survives substrate change, even though Miller's specific
7±2 does not.

C also illustrates a second-order point about how to read
the figure: /a direction's tier is not its primary
finding's tier/. CLT (B) has intrinsic / extraneous load at
behaviour level and germane load at substrate level — two
tiers in one theory. C has the structural-form claim at
behaviour level and Miller's number at substrate level —
again two tiers. The transfer-gradient figure plots /the
finding's centre of mass/, not a single point — partial-
transfer directions are partial because they straddle the
horizontal divide.

* D. Eye-tracking and saccade
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D
:END:

Direction D is where the substrate gap looks largest. Human
reading is a /motor/ process: 200–250-millisecond fixations
punctuated by 7–9-character saccades, regressions back to
mis-parsed words, parafoveal preview of upcoming text. Keith
Rayner's 1998 review "Eye Movements in Reading and
Information Processing: 20 Years of Research" (=Psychological
Bulletin= 124(3), pp. 372–422) consolidated the empirical
field. Marcel Just and Patricia Carpenter's 1980 "A Theory of
Reading: From Eye Fixations to Comprehension" (=Psychological
Review= 87(4), pp. 329–354) proposed the /eye-mind hypothesis/
— the eye remains fixated on a word as long as that word is
being processed. The E-Z Reader cognitive-control model (Reichle, Pollatsek,
Fisher & Rayner 1998 onward) is the dominant computational
account. AI agents have no eyes, no fixations, no saccades:
D sits in the /lower-right human-only quadrant/ of the
figure together with [[#dir-H][H. Gestalt principles]], its findings
load-bearing only for the human reader.

** D.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-1
:END:

Five quantified claims define the field. The /eye-mind
hypothesis/ (Just & Carpenter 1980): the eye remains fixated
on a word as long as the word is being cognitively processed,
so fixation duration is a direct measure of processing time.
The /perceptual span/ (Rayner 1998 review): about fourteen
to fifteen characters extend to the right of fixation in
left-to-right scripts, only three to four to the left, with
the asymmetry reflecting reading direction. /Regressions/
account for roughly ten to fifteen percent of fixations
under normal reading and rise sharply when comprehension
fails. Mean /saccade length/ is seven to nine characters
for skilled adult readers of English; the figure varies by
script (Chinese, with denser information per character, has
shorter saccades). /Parafoveal preview/ — the partial
processing of the next word before the saccade lands on it
— reduces fixation duration on that next word by an average
of thirty to fifty milliseconds.

** D.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-2
:END:

Sixty years of high-precision eye-tracking data sit behind
the field. Rayner's 1998 =Psychological Bulletin= review
([[https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.124.3.372][DOI 10.1037/0033-2909.124.3.372]]) consolidated several
hundred studies into a coherent quantitative picture of
fixation, saccade, regression, and perceptual-span
behaviour. Just & Carpenter 1980 in =Psychological Review=
87(4) ([[https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.87.4.329][DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.87.4.329]]) proposed the
eye-mind hypothesis on the basis of fixation-duration
regression analyses against word characteristics (the
model accounts for 72% of variance in word gaze
durations). The E-Z Reader cognitive-control model
(Reichle, Pollatsek, Fisher & Rayner 1998 in =Psychological
Review= 105(1), pp. 125–157;
[[https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.105.1.125][DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.105.1.125]], plus a decade of
subsequent papers) provides a serial-attention computational
account. The SWIFT model (Engbert, Nuthmann, Richter &
Kliegl 2005 in =Psychological Review= 112(4), pp. 777–813;
[[https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.112.4.777][DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.112.4.777]]) competes with E-Z
Reader using a parallel-processing approach; the two models
continue to be tested against new eye-tracking datasets. Rayner's 2009 =Quarterly Journal of Experimental
Psychology= 62(8) review "Eye Movements and Attention in
Reading, Scene Perception, and Visual Search" extended the
quantitative summary to non-reading tasks.

** D.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-3
:END:

Agents have no eyes, but functional analogues exist for
some of the five claims. /Re-reading via repeated Read
tool-calls/ on the same file when initial interpretation was
insufficient is the closest analogue of regression — same
behaviour (return to earlier material when comprehension
fails), different mechanism (re-issued tool call vs
saccade). /Variable attention weight/ within a single
forward pass on different tokens loosely echoes fixation-
duration variability, but only as an architectural curiosity
— the agent does not "spend time" on heavy tokens in the
biological sense. /Retrieval-augmented look-ahead/ —
agents that fetch related context before tackling a complex
section — is a deliberate, programmable substitute for
parafoveal preview. The eye-mind hypothesis itself does not
transfer: there is no "fixation duration" in token
processing, and the architectural primitive (attention
weight) is shaped by training, not by within-task
cognitive effort.

** D.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-4
:END:

Only one weak primitive transfers cleanly: /processing
effort scales with item-level complexity/. Both human
readers and AI agents spend more on hard items than easy
ones — fixation duration rises on rare words, technical
jargon, syntactically unusual constructions; analogously,
agents allocate more attention mass and (in chain-of-
thought scaffolding) more reasoning tokens to hard items.
Both populations backtrack when later context disambiguates
earlier interpretation. The constraint shape rhymes — but
this is the weakest of the four "shared primitive"
findings in the document so far. The substrate divergence
is so large that calling this a "transfer" rather than a
"vague resemblance" depends on charity.

** D.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-5
:END:

The transfer breaks almost completely. The /perceptual-span
asymmetry/ (more to the right of fixation than to the left
in English readers, reversed for Hebrew or Arabic readers)
reflects retinal acuity and reading direction — facts about
the eye and the script that an agent has no analogue for.
The eye-mind hypothesis assumes /serial/ visual processing
the agent does not perform: token streams enter the model
in parallel within a single forward pass, with no temporal
order analogous to the saccade sequence. EZ Reader's model
parameters — lexical access time, attention shift cost,
oculomotor programming time — are all motor- and biological-
substrate quantities with no token-stream equivalents. Of
all ten directions in this document, D has the largest
substrate gap between human and agent processing. The
direction's value for LP-with-AI is /negative knowledge/ —
knowing which design moves serve only the human reader.

** D.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-6
:END:

The takeaway is /human-only optimisation/. Three practices
that eye-tracking research validates for the human reader:
/short paragraphs/ (reduce regression chance when
comprehension fails mid-paragraph), /front-loaded sentences/
(parafoveal preview captures the important content), and
/consistent typographic cues/ (reduce uncertainty about
where the next saccade should land). None of these benefits
the agent reader — paragraph length, sentence-start position,
typographic consistency all wash out in token processing.
The dual-audience implication is documented at
[[#dir-J-6][J.6]]: load-bearing signifiers belong in the structural
layer (=CUSTOM_ID=, =#+NAME:=), and the typographic-only
optimisations recorded here serve humans only.

** D.7 Synthesis: D in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-7
:END:

Direction D occupies the /lower-right human-only quadrant/
of [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]] together with [[#dir-H][H. Gestalt principles]]. D
contributes the cleanest case in the catalogue of a finding
whose every empirical layer is biological-apparatus
specific: Rayner 1998's review reports saccade lengths in
degrees of visual angle, fixation durations in milliseconds
of foveal sampling, regression rates as percentages of
re-fixated words; Just & Carpenter 1980's eye-mind
assumption explicitly identifies the eye position with the
locus of comprehension; Reichle, Pollatsek, Fisher & Rayner
1998's E-Z Reader model parameterises everything against
parafoveal preview and saccade-target selection. There is
no agent analogue to any of these substrates.

D clarifies the /human-only quadrant's internal structure/.
It is a member of two shared-primitive families
([[#synthesis-families][synthesis-families]]):

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where D sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-C][C. Working memory limits]], [[#dir-H][H. Gestalt principles]] | /Substrate-bound numerical constants/ | D contributes Rayner saccade-length distributions and parafoveal-span pixel ranges; like C, the numerical layer is what fails to transfer |
| [[#dir-H][H. Gestalt principles]] | /Perceptual-substrate-only/ | D contributes saccade / fixation / regression mechanisms; H contributes perceptual grouping; both have /no upper-quadrant projection at all/ |

The distinction between the two families is whether
stripping the numbers leaves a portable claim:

- C's "capacity is bounded; chunking mitigates" survives
  Miller-number removal and ports to agent design.
- D's "saccades land on word centres; parafoveal preview
  shapes fixation duration" does not survive saccade
  removal — saccades /are/ the finding, not a substrate
  parameter inside a finding.
- H's "grouping arises from proximity" similarly cannot be
  stripped of perception and still mean anything for an
  agent reader.

The result is a two-layer figure structure for the lower
quadrants: /Substrate-bound numerical constants/ runs
across the partial-low and human-only stripes (members
have an LP-portable upper-quadrant projection of varying
strength); /Perceptual-substrate-only/ runs only across the
human-only stripe (members have no portable projection at
all).

D's relationship with [[#dir-J][J. Affordances and signifiers]]
illustrates a particularly clear cross-direction projection.
J's /transfer break at typographic signifiers/ (font
weight, italics, whitespace, colour-only emphasis collapse
to flat tokens for agents) is precisely the LP-design
projection of D's perceptual-substrate limit. A human
reader picks up a bold-faced word through D-substrate
processes (foveal sampling resolves the bolded glyph as
distinctively darker, parafoveal preview directs the saccade
toward it earlier); an agent sees just =word=. The operational
rule for J — /load-bearing affordances must be structural
signifiers, not typographic ones/ — derives mechanistically
from D's substrate limit. D names what the human-only
perceptual apparatus does that agents cannot; J names the
LP design rule that follows.

* E. Mental models and dual-process
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E
:END:

Direction E is the philosophically loaded direction —
"mental model" is a term used differently by three traditions
that all converge in the HCI literature. Philip Johnson-Laird
formalised /mental models/ in cognitive psychology with his
1983 book =Mental Models: Towards a Cognitive Science of
Language, Inference, and Consciousness= (Harvard University
Press, 528 pages), arguing the mind builds inner replicas
that ground comprehension and inference. Donald Norman
borrowed the term for HCI in =The Design of Everyday Things=
(1988, revised 2013), distinguishing the /designer's model/,
the /system image/, and the /user's mental model/, and noting
that mismatch among the three is the dominant source of
usability failure. Daniel Kahneman synthesised four decades of dual-process
research in =Thinking, Fast and Slow= (Farrar, Straus &
Giroux 2011), naming System 1 (fast, automatic, associative)
and System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical) as the two
reasoning modes — labels first coined in Stanovich & West
2000 =Behavioral and Brain Sciences= 23(5). E sits on the
/upper-left to middle-left boundary/ of the figure: the
dual-process half transfers cleanly (chain-of-thought vs
fast inference is the structural analogue), the
mental-model half straddles the [[#synthesis-families][cross-session state
persistence]] break.

** E.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-1
:END:

Three claims travel under the same banner. /Johnson-Laird/:
reasoning works by /manipulating mental models/ — analog
representations of the situation being reasoned about — not
by applying formal syntactic rules; people who get a logic
problem right are usually building and testing models
mentally, not running through axioms. /Norman/: usability
failures track the /gap between the designer's model and the
user's model/, mediated by the /system image/ (what the
artefact communicates about itself); when the gap is wide,
users construct wrong predictions about behaviour and the
artefact appears broken even when it works as designed.
/Kahneman/: human reasoning runs in /two modes/ — System 1
(fast, automatic, associative, low-cost) and System 2 (slow,
deliberate, analytical, expensive), with distinct biases and
distinct resource profiles. The three claims converge in HCI
practice: design the system image so the user's mental model
matches the designer's, and respect when System 2 deliberation
is required vs when System 1 pattern-matching suffices.

** E.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-2
:END:

Each strand carries its own empirical record. /Johnson-Laird/:
roughly four decades of syllogism and conditional-reasoning
experiments showing that error rates predict from
mental-model construction time, not from premise complexity
per se. The 1983 book =Mental Models= consolidated the
programme; Byrne 2005 =The Rational Imagination= extended it
to counterfactuals. /Norman/: =The Design of Everyday Things=
1988 was largely observational with empirical follow-ups in
later editions; the 1986 edited volume =User Centered System
Design= (Norman & Draper) provided the academic foundation.
/Kahneman/: Tversky & Kahneman 1974 in =Science= 185(4157)
launched the heuristics-and-biases programme; the 1979
prospect-theory paper in =Econometrica= 47(2), pp. 263–292
applied the framework to decision under risk and earned the
2002 Nobel; dozens of replications across forty years build
the dual-process literature (Evans 2008 =Annual Review of
Psychology= 59 review; Stanovich 2011 =Rationality and the
Reflective Mind=).

** E.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-3
:END:

The isomorphism is selective. /Mental models within a single
inference/: yes — the agent maintains activation patterns
that function as a working model of the current task, and
that working model predicts subsequent token choices. /Mental
models across sessions/: no — there is no durable
consolidation absent retrieval augmentation. /Designer's-model
vs user's-model gap/: yes, and obviously — when LP prose
communicates a different mental model than the code embodies,
both human and agent readers fail in similar ways (the agent
produces edits inconsistent with the code's actual semantics;
the human submits PRs that surprise the maintainer). /System
1 vs System 2/: the mapping is suggestive — fast-path LLM
inference is associative pattern completion (System 1
analogue), chain-of-thought prompting is the deliberate
analogue (System 2 analogue), and chain-of-thought visibly
helps on hard problems the same way deliberation helps
humans. But the architectural mechanism (additional inference
steps) is unlike biological System 2 (recruitment of
prefrontal cortex / working memory).

** E.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-4
:END:

Two shared primitives, distinct in source. First,
/reasoning quality scales with deliberation cost/. Both
humans and agents do better on hard problems when they
spend more "steps" — Kahneman's System 2 in humans, chain-
of-thought reasoning tokens in agents — and worse when
forced to answer fast. The functional curve is the same
even though the substrate (recruitment of prefrontal
attention vs additional inference passes) differs entirely.
Second, /designer's-model vs user's-model gap as failure
predictor/. Both human users and AI agents fail when the
prose communicates a different model than the code embodies.
This is Norman's claim restated for documents — and it
transfers cleanly because the failure mode does not depend
on the user's cognitive architecture, only on the
informational asymmetry between what the document says and
what the code does.

** E.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-5
:END:

Two break points. /Johnson-Laird's mental-models theory/
makes specific claims about /spatial/ and /token/ analog
representations — humans reason about syllogisms by
constructing imagined arrangements of objects, not by
parsing sentences. LLMs have no analog representational
substrate; their "mental models" exist only as activation
patterns over tokens. The transfer breaks on representation
type even when the functional inference outcome looks
similar. /Kahneman's biases catalogue/ (anchoring,
availability, representativeness, framing) is empirically
derived from human-cognition experiments. LLMs show
/different/ biases (positional bias per Liu et al. 2024;
token-frequency bias; sycophancy per Perez et al. 2022;
recency bias on long contexts) that rhyme structurally but
are not the same items as the human catalogue. Anyone
applying Kahneman's specific bias list to LLMs gets a
partial fit at best.

** E.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-6
:END:

Two practices recurrent in dual-audience LP carry strong
returns. First, /make the designer's mental model explicit/.
LP prose preambles that state the trade-off the code
embodies, the rejected alternative, and the load-bearing
invariant reduce reverse-engineering for both human and
agent readers. Norman's claim transfers verbatim: the LP
doc is the /system image/ of the codebase; the wider the
gap between what it communicates and what the code does,
the more confusion both audiences produce. Second, /signal
where deliberation is needed/. Complex sections that frame
"this section requires careful reasoning" for humans and
embed explicit chain-of-thought scaffolding for agents
(numbered steps, named intermediate facts, "first... then..."
structure) avoid the failure mode where System 2 is
demanded of a human while the agent slides past in a single
forward pass — a recipe for agent-introduced bugs.

** E.7 Synthesis: E in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-7
:END:

Direction E sits on the /upper-left to middle-left
boundary/ of [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]] — a partial-high transfer. E
braids two canonical findings (Norman 1988 mental models;
Kahneman 2011 System 1 / System 2) with different transfer
profiles, and its synthesis subsection unbraids them.

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where E sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-F][F. Recognition vs recall]], [[#dir-G][G. Progressive disclosure]] | /Dual-process processing modes/ | E contributes the named theoretical frame; F supplies the recognition-side optimisation; G the deliberation-side scaffolding |
| [[#dir-B][B. Cognitive load theory]], [[#dir-G][G. Progressive disclosure]], [[#dir-I][I. Berry-picking]] | /Cross-session state persistence/ | E's mental-model accretion (Norman 1988's "user's model" builds over repeated encounters) breaks at the agent's stateless cold-start, the same boundary as B's germane load |

Evidence that the agent shows the dual-process structural-
form analogue lives in observed behaviour: an agent
skimming a long file via grep + small
Read windows operates in a fast pattern-matching mode that
rhymes with Kahneman's System 1 (rapid, scent-driven,
heuristic, error-prone); the same agent asked to "explain
why this refactor preserves the invariant" produces longer
chain-of-thought traces with explicit intermediate steps
that rhyme with System 2 (slow, deliberate, sequentially
constructed, costly). The /Anthropic skill SDK/'s explicit
support for /extended-thinking blocks/ and =CLAUDE.md='s
"every claim backed by tool output" rule are both ports of
the System-2-scaffolding move into agent practice. The
shared primitive is /two distinct processing modes
selectable by stake/, not /dopaminergic-amygdala-PFC
neuroanatomy/ which is human-only.

E's Norman-1988 half projects onto the /Cross-session state
persistence/ family — the same family B sits in. The
user's mental model of the system is canonically described
as /accreting through repeated use/: misconceptions develop
in one encounter, get corrected in the next, eventually
settle into a stable representation that lets the user
predict system behaviour. Agents have no analogue of this
between-session accretion. Within-session model building
(an agent reading a codebase and developing a working
representation of it within one conversation) transfers
cleanly; cross-session model accretion does not.

E thus instantiates /one direction in two families/: its
dual-process half lives in the upper-left full-transfer
zone, its mental-models half straddles the persistence-
break boundary. Together with B, E is one of two clean
examples of a single theoretical direction whose components
project onto different figure cells — a pattern that argues
for /component-level/ rather than /direction-level/
transfer assessment ([[#synthesis-observations][synthesis-observations]] item 6).

* F. Recognition vs recall
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F
:END:

Direction F is one of HCI's most-quoted asymmetries: human
recognition is dramatically easier than human recall.
Endel Tulving and Donald Thomson formalised the phenomenon in
"Encoding Specificity and Retrieval Processes in Episodic
Memory" (=Psychological Review= 80(5), 1973, pp. 352–373) —
retrieval succeeds only when retrieval cues overlap with
encoding context, so providing the right cue (recognition)
transforms hard recall into easy access. Jakob Nielsen made
this the sixth of his ten usability heuristics (1994
formulation): /"Recognition rather than recall — minimise the
user's memory load by making elements, actions, and options
visible."/ Three decades of UI design have absorbed the finding:
command palettes, autocomplete dropdowns, TOCs, file pickers,
and breadcrumbs all replace recall with recognition. F sits
in the /upper-left full-transfer quadrant/ of the figure and
is the catalogue's /cross-family hub/ — a member of both
[[#synthesis-families][Foraging-economics]] (A, F, I) and [[#synthesis-families][Dual-process processing
modes]] (E, F, G).

** F.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-1
:END:

Two claims, one direct, one institutional. Tulving and
Thomson's /encoding-specificity principle/ states that a
retrieval cue is effective only to the degree it overlaps
with the encoding context — the very same item is easier to
retrieve when surrounded by features present at encoding
than when surrounded by unfamiliar context. The behavioural
consequence is the recognition-recall gap: in classic
free-recall vs recognition experiments on the same word lists,
recognition rates routinely exceed eighty percent while
free-recall rates stay near twenty percent. Nielsen's
sixth usability heuristic encodes the design implication for
HCI: /"Recognition rather than recall — minimise the user's
memory load by making elements, actions, and options visible.
The user should not have to remember information from one
part of the interface to another. Information required to
use the design (e.g., field labels or menu items) should be
visible or easily retrievable when needed."/

** F.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-2
:END:

Tulving & Thomson 1973 in =Psychological Review= 80(5)
presented three experiments demonstrating encoding-specificity
with word-pair studies — subjects encoded target words in the
context of weak associates, then attempted recall with either
matching weak-associate cues or with stronger but
unmatched-at-encoding cues; the matching weak cues
out-performed the unmatched strong cues, a counter-intuitive
finding that established the principle. Tulving's 1983 book
=Elements of Episodic Memory= consolidated the programme and
introduced the episodic-vs-semantic memory distinction.
Nielsen's heuristic-evaluation programme began with
Nielsen & Molich 1990 (nine heuristics in =CHI '90=) and was
refined to ten in Nielsen 1994 chapter in =Usability
Inspection Methods=. The institutional consequence in HCI
was immediate: by the late 1990s every commercial UI
review-process listed "recognition over recall" among its
top five evaluation criteria.

** F.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-3
:END:

The isomorphism is one of the strongest in the document.
Agents perform dramatically better when the relevant content
is /already in the context window/ (recognition-analogue)
than when asked to generate facts unprompted from training-
data alone (recall-analogue). Hallucination — confident
generation of incorrect facts — is literally what failed
recall looks like in a generative system; the agent fills
the prompt-shaped gap with the most contextually-plausible
content, which often is not correct. The entire
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) architecture is the
industry's collective admission that recognition beats recall
in LLMs too: if you can put the right document chunk in front
of the model, accuracy rises by orders of magnitude over
asking the model to recall the same content. The =CLAUDE.md=
"Tool-Use Protocol" — "every claim backed by tool output" —
is institutionally the same insight as Nielsen heuristic #6
re-derived for agents.

** F.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-4
:END:

The shared primitive is one of the cleanest in the document:
/performance on retrieval tasks is dominated by cue
availability, not by storage adequacy/. Both populations
"know" far more than they can freely produce. Both benefit
massively from having the right cue visible at the moment of
retrieval. The cue itself differs by substrate — a
perceptual stimulus for humans, a context-window token for
agents — but the operational consequence is identical:
design the surface to provide cues, not to test recall.
Hallucination and human forgetting are the failure mode of
the same constraint encountered under different
implementations. This is one of three full-transfer findings
in the document (with A foraging and I berry-picking).

** F.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-5
:END:

Two break points. First, /memory-system separation/: humans
distinguish working memory, long-term declarative memory,
episodic memory (Tulving 1972), procedural memory, etc., each
with distinct retrieval properties. Agents have no such
separation — pre-training weights, fine-tuning weights,
context-window content, and retrieval-augmented lookups all
contribute to output, but there is no architectural analogue
of the human episodic/semantic distinction. Second, the
/encoding-specificity claim/ — that retrieval cue
effectiveness depends on encoding context — has no clean
LLM analogue because the model was not "encoded" in any
episodic sense. What looks superficially like encoding
specificity (the LLM does better when prompted in a register
similar to its training data) is more accurately
prompt-template bias or position bias under a different
name. The recognition-recall gap itself transfers; the
specific mechanism Tulving identified for human recall
failure does not.

** F.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-6
:END:

The recognition-vs-recall asymmetry is one of the cleanest
transfers in the document and yields the strongest
dual-audience design moves. Three practices follow directly.
First, /LP prose preambles that provide the cue/ make the
code block intelligible without forcing the reader to recall
how this section relates to the prior one, or the agent to
remember the file's top-level invariant. Second,
/cross-references are pure recognition surfaces/ —
=CUSTOM_ID= anchors, "see also" links, named-block
references, and dense TOCs all replace recall with
recognition. Third, /the failure mode is symmetric/:
hallucination in agents and "I knew that but couldn't find
it" in humans are the same constraint encountered under
different substrates; the mitigation — make the cue visible
— is identical. F is the direction where HCI heuristics
transfer least changed; Nielsen's 1994 wording reads as-is
for an LLM-era prompt-engineering guide.

** F.7 Synthesis: F as cross-family hub
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-F-7
:END:

Direction F is the /cross-family hub/ of [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]]. F
sits in the upper-left full-transfer quadrant as a member
of two distinct shared-primitive families —
[[#synthesis-families][Foraging-economics]] (A, F, I) and [[#synthesis-families][Dual-process
processing modes]] (E, F, G). Membership in both is a
substantive structural claim, not a naming convenience: the
recognition-vs-recall asymmetry /is/ what makes both
foraging and dual-process work mechanistically.

Tulving & Thomson's 1973 encoding-specificity finding maps
onto agent retrieval with mechanical precision. The 1973
paper showed that recall accuracy depends on overlap
between encoding-time context and retrieval-time cues — a
finding so substrate-bound (discovered through human memory
experiments) that one would expect it to break. It does
not: in the agent setting, grep retrieval depends on overlap
between the keyword the agent types and the actual tokens
stored in the file. /Recognition/ is what happens when the
cue is in the search-results page already (filename, snippet,
heading); /recall/ is what happens when the agent must
generate the keyword from a prior internal representation.
Both populations succeed more often at the former.

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where F sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-A][A. Information foraging]], [[#dir-I][I. Berry-picking]] | /Foraging-economics/ | F supplies the recognition-side asymmetry that makes scent-driven patch selection rational — cheap proximal cues = recognition cues |
| [[#dir-E][E. Mental models + dual-process]], [[#dir-G][G. Progressive disclosure]] | /Dual-process processing modes/ | F's recognition surface is what System 1 reads; F's recall demand is what System 2 has to perform when recognition fails; G provides the staged-recognition scaffolding |

The cross-family hub status explains /why/ the upper-left
quadrant has three members rather than one. A (foraging),
F (recognition), and I (berry-picking) are not three
independent findings that coincidentally all transfer
cleanly — they are three facets of the same underlying
constraint structure. Pirolli-Card's "scent" is recognition-
side information (the proximal cue); Bates' berry-picking
is iterative recognition (each retrieved item updates which
cues count as scent); Tulving's encoding-specificity is the
precise mechanism explaining why scent works at all
(retrieval succeeds when cue and stored representation
overlap).

The dual-audience LP design rule that derives from this
hub is the cleanest in the catalogue: /externalise every
cue that the reader would otherwise have to recall/. This
serves the human reader (Nielsen heuristic #6, 1995/2020),
the agent reader (grep success requires cue token to match
stored token), and the System-2 form for both populations
(deliberate retrieval costs both populations more than
recognition, by orders of magnitude in token budget for
agents and in fixation-time for humans). The host project's
=naming-conventions.md= /Variable role-encoding/ rule
("would a newcomer know what this holds from the name
alone?") and =literate-programming-document-first.md/'s
heading-as-concept rule are both ports of F's cue-
externalisation move into LP practice.

* G. Progressive disclosure and scaffolded UI
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G
:END:

Direction G names the design pattern of /revealing
information or options on demand rather than upfront/.
Jakob Nielsen's NN/g article "Progressive Disclosure"
([[https://www.nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure/][nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure]], 4 December
2006) gives the operational definition: /"defer secondary
options to a subsidiary screen, focusing users' attention
on the primary options."/ Bruce Tognazzini's =Tog on
Interface= (Addison-Wesley 1992) established the related
/moment-of-need/ discipline — surface controls and
information when the user is positioned to use them, not
earlier or later. Steve Krug's =Don't Make Me Think= (2000)
operationalised progressive disclosure as one of three
usability laws for the web. Jeroen van Merriënboer's 4C/ID
/whole-task variation/ adds a tutorial-design layer: vary
the complexity of practice tasks in a graduated sequence so
each new layer extends without overwhelming. G sits on the
/partial-high right side/ of the figure: decision-time
disclosure transfers to AI agents cleanly, skill-building
disclosure breaks at the agent's stateless cold-start.

** G.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-1
:END:

The core claim has four near-synonymous formulations.
Nielsen 1995: /"Defer secondary options to a subsidiary
screen. The initial view should focus on the primary
options; secondary options should be available on request
but should not be visible by default."/ Tognazzini's
/moment-of-need/ framing in =Tog on Interface= (1992):
controls and information should be surfaced when the user
is positioned to use them, not earlier (clutters) or later
(too late). Krug 2000: every screen should be
self-explanatory /at its own level of detail/, so progressing
deeper reveals more without requiring the user to remember
context from upstream screens. Van Merriënboer's /whole-task
variation/ in 4C/ID adds the learning-design layer: vary the
complexity of practice tasks in a graduated sequence so each
new layer extends without overwhelming.

** G.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-2
:END:

The empirical foundation is mostly industrial-case-study
rather than controlled-experiment. Nielsen's 1995
formulation was followed by case studies in NN/g consulting
reports through the late 1990s and early 2000s, repeatedly
showing that interfaces with progressive disclosure reduced
error rates and increased task completion among non-expert
users. Apple's macOS and iOS device-onboarding flows are
the canonical commercial implementation. TurboTax wizards
illustrate the pattern in domain-complex software (tax
returns); they replace a comprehensive form with a sequence
of small disclosures triggered by prior answers, and have
been credited with the product's market dominance.
Van Merriënboer 1997 and 2017 contain the experimental
foundation for /whole-task variation/ in instructional
design — graduated-complexity practice tasks beat blocked
practice on transfer tests in studies of complex skills
acquisition (medical decision-making, troubleshooting,
programming).

** G.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-3
:END:

The transfer is asymmetric but real. Agents have far larger
/context windows/ than humans have /working memory/, so the
cost of "showing everything at once" is lower in absolute
terms — but it is /not zero/. Token budget consumption,
positional bias (Liu et al. 2024 "Lost in the Middle"), and
attention dilution mean that progressive disclosure still
helps agents: irrelevant content in the context window
demonstrably degrades downstream output even when there is
budget to hold it. The canonical agent-side example is the
=.claude/skills/*= front-matter system: =when_to_use=,
=paths=, and =disable-model-invocation= fields function as a
disclosure gate. The skill body is surfaced into agent
context only when the trigger conditions match. This is
progressive disclosure /verbatim/ — Nielsen 1995 wording
applies to skills front-matter unmodified. The Anthropic
skill SDK was not derived from progressive-disclosure
literature (or if it was, no public discussion mentions it),
but the convergent design is striking.

** G.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-4
:END:

The shared primitive: /attention is a scarce resource
regardless of substrate, and irrelevant content competes
for that resource even when there is room to hold it/.
Whether the budget is biological working memory (4±1
chunks) or transformer context window (~200K tokens),
surfacing content that is not immediately load-bearing
costs something — discrimination effort for the human,
positional dilution for the agent. Progressive disclosure
transfers because it respects this shared resource
constraint: surface what serves the current decision, defer
what serves a later one. The budget sizes differ by orders
of magnitude but the structural intent — match disclosed
content to the reader's current need — applies identically.
This is one of the directions where the human/agent
analogy holds even after we set aside the substrate detail.

** G.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-5
:END:

The break is in the /learning-over-time/ part. Van
Merriënboer's 4C/ID whole-task variation relies on /durable
skill acquisition across sessions/ — humans build expertise
through repeated practice with varied surface forms,
consolidating schemas that survive between sessions. Agents
do not build durable skills inside a single inference;
they either know-from-training (fine-tuning is out of band)
or they retrieve, but they do not "practice" within a
conversation. The /4C/ part of 4C/ID — Learning Tasks /
Supportive Info / Procedural Info / Part-task Practice —
maps onto LP doc structure only loosely. The
/disclosure-of-current-information/ aspect transfers
cleanly (G.4); the /sequencing-for-skill-building/ aspect
does not. This is a clearer instance of the same break we
saw in direction B (germane load) and direction E (durable
mental models) — anything that depends on consolidation
between sessions breaks at the agent boundary.

** G.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-6
:END:

Three practices follow with strong dual-audience returns.
First, /progressive disclosure operates at multiple scales/:
the host project's =CLAUDE.md= → =.claude/skills/*= →
skill-body cascade is a three-tier disclosure structure for
agents; the README → ARCHITECTURE.org → module-detail
cascade is the human analogue. The two cascades share
intermediate layers when the LP author chooses
section-as-concept headings. Second, /name the trigger
explicitly/. SKILL.md =when_to_use= fields are
progressive-disclosure triggers written in natural language;
the human equivalent is the opening sentence of a section
that names when this section is the relevant one. Third,
/two disclosure purposes split/. /Decision-time disclosure/
(surface what the reader needs right now) transfers
cleanly; /skill-building disclosure/ (sequence content for
learning) does not transfer to agents and remains a human-
only optimisation when present.

** G.7 Synthesis: G as second cross-family hub
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-7
:END:

Direction G sits on the /partial-high right side/ of
[[#synthesis-figure][the figure]] as the catalogue's second cross-family hub
after F. G is a member of two existing families and the
canonical case-study for /convergent design without
lineage/ ([[#synthesis-observations][synthesis-observations]] item 2):

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where G sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-E][E. Mental models + dual-process]], [[#dir-F][F. Recognition vs recall]] | /Dual-process processing modes/ | G provides the deliberation-side scaffolding that lets System 2 activate; tutorials, examples, named-step lists, =when_to_use= cues all manage System 1 → System 2 hand-off |
| [[#dir-B][B. Cognitive load theory]], [[#dir-E][E. Mental models]], [[#dir-I][I. Berry-picking]] | /Cross-session state persistence/ | G's expert-recognition payoff — Carroll 1990's /production paradox/ where experts skip tutorials they once needed — depends on across-session memory of "I learned this once"; agent has no such memory |

The /Anthropic skill SDK/'s =when_to_use= field — a
one-paragraph natural-language description prepended to
every skill describing when the skill should be invoked —
re-implements Carroll & Rosson 1987's "minimalist
instruction" doctrine and Krug 2000's "Don't Make Me Think"
first-screen heuristic /without/ any documented derivation
from either source. The skill-SDK designers did not start
from progressive-disclosure literature; they started from
"agents waste budget reading irrelevant skills" and
converged on the same shape. Similarly, the =allowed-tools:=
front-matter field re-implements Tognazzini's
progressive-disclosure scaffolding (limit available actions
until the relevant step) without referencing the 1992
=Tog on Interface= where Tognazzini named the doctrine.

The structural explanation: both populations face the same
interface constraint — bounded budget + heterogeneous
candidate items + fast triage at entry — and any rational
design under those constraints lands within a small region
of design space. All three canonical authors (Tognazzini
for HCI, Carroll for minimalist instruction, Krug for web
design) explicitly named the same constraint and arrived at
variations of the same answer; the Anthropic skill SDK
arrived at it again from agent-side data. Four independent
populations (HCI users / minimalist-instruction learners /
web readers / AI agents) converged on the same design
family because the interface constraint is universal even
though the populations are unrelated.

G's two-purpose disclosure split (G.6) — /decision-time/
vs /skill-building/ — is a direct instance of the
cross-session persistence break: the decision-time variant
transfers cleanly to agents (=when_to_use= and tool-
allowlists are its members); the skill-building variant
does not because skill is durable cross-session state.
Same direction, two components, two figure cells —
component-level transfer assessment per
[[#synthesis-observations][synthesis-observations]] item 6.

* H. Gestalt principles
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H
:END:

Direction H is the oldest direction in this document. Max
Wertheimer founded Gestalt psychology with the 1912 phi-
phenomenon paper and consolidated the perceptual-grouping
principles in "Untersuchungen zur Lehre von der Gestalt II"
(=Psychologische Forschung= 4, 1923, pp. 301–350), known in
English as "Laws of Organization in Perceptual Form." The
principles describe how the human visual system groups
discrete stimuli into unified percepts: /proximity/ (nearby
items group), /similarity/ (similar items group),
/closure/ (incomplete figures perceived as complete),
/continuation/ (collinear items perceived as continuous),
/common fate/ (items moving together group), and
/figure-ground/ (objects emerge against backgrounds). A
century of vision-science research has validated and refined
the principles (Palmer 1999 =Vision Science=; Wagemans et al.
2012 century-review in =Psychological Bulletin=). Document layout — whitespace, indentation, typographic
hierarchy, related-item grouping — exploits Gestalt
principles continuously. H sits in the /lower-right
human-only quadrant/ of the figure alongside [[#dir-D][D]]: the
substrate gap to agent token streams is total, and H's
findings load-bearing only for the human reader.

** H.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-1
:END:

Six classical principles plus an organising super-principle.
/Proximity/: items spatially close tend to be perceived as
grouped. /Similarity/: items sharing visual properties
(colour, shape, size) tend to be grouped. /Closure/:
incomplete figures are perceived as complete (the partial
circle "closes" perceptually). /Continuation/ (also "good
continuation"): collinear or smoothly-curving items are
perceived as continuous. /Common fate/: items moving in
sync are perceived as a group. /Figure-ground/: the visual
field decomposes into a foreground figure and a background.
The umbrella principle is /Prägnanz/ — "good figure" — the
claim that perception organises stimuli into the simplest,
most stable, most regular available interpretation. The
six principles are not independent axioms but specific
manifestations of Prägnanz operating across different
stimulus dimensions.

** H.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-2
:END:

A century of vision-science work. Wertheimer 1923 is the
foundation paper, derived from earlier work on apparent
motion (the 1912 phi-phenomenon paper). Edgar Rubin's
=Visuell wahrgenommene Figuren= (1915) introduced the
figure-ground reversal demonstrations (the "Rubin vase"
example). Wolfgang Köhler 1929 =Gestalt Psychology= and
Kurt Koffka 1935 =Principles of Gestalt Psychology= gave
the school its textbook form. Modern quantitative work
includes Kubovy & van den Berg 2008 on proximity-grouping
strength (=Psychological Review= 115(1), pp. 131–154,
formal lattice-grouping model), Wagemans et al. 2012
=Psychological Bulletin= 138(6) two-part century review
covering perceptual grouping (Part I) and figure-ground
(Part II). Neurological work has localised parts of the
processing — V1 receives stimulus information, but figure-
ground is computed in V2 and beyond (Lamme 1995
=Journal of Neuroscience= 15(2), pp. 1605–1615).

** H.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-3
:END:

The transfer is largely absent. Agents do not process 2D
visual fields — they process token streams. /Proximity in
2D space/ has no agent analogue; tokens have sequence
position but no spatial proximity along orthogonal
dimensions. /Closure/, /continuation/, and /common fate/
all rely on visual-perception primitives the agent lacks
entirely. Two principles have weak token-stream rhymes.
/Similarity/: agents attend more to tokens with similar
embedding-space representations within a context — the
attention head's dot-product similarity is the structural
analogue, with documented effects (induction heads,
attention-based pattern matching). /Figure-ground/:
salient tokens with high attention weight stand out against
the background of low-weight tokens — analogous to how
high-frequency or recently-activated content dominates
generation. Neither rhyme is precise; they are
mathematical analogies one level removed from the visual
phenomena Wertheimer described.

** H.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-4
:END:

A weak shared primitive at one level: /processing groups
co-occurring items/. The human visual system groups by
spatial proximity, similarity, and the other Gestalt
factors. The transformer attention system groups by
sequence proximity (recency, locality of attention windows)
and embedding-space proximity (similar tokens cluster in
attention). The mathematical primitive — clustering nearby
items into joint processing — may be deeper than the visual
manifestation, in which case the structural principle is
substrate-agnostic. But this stretches the term "shared
primitive" past its useful limit; calling token attention
"Gestalt-like" is metaphorical, not isomorphic. H is the
weakest-transfer direction in the document together with D.

** H.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-5
:END:

The break is in the /visual-specificity/ of the principles.
Wertheimer's experiments used 2D visual stimuli — dot
arrays, line segments, motion sequences, figure-ground
ambiguity (Rubin vase). The six principles describe how
human visual cortex resolves competing organisation
hypotheses for visual input. Token-stream processing has no
visual cortex, no 2D space, no acuity gradient, no motion.
Closure (visual completion of incomplete figures) presupposes
a perceptual machinery that fills gaps in a visual array;
agents do not fill perceptual gaps because they have no
perceptual array to fill. Continuation (good curve)
presupposes the visual primitive of curvature. Common fate
presupposes time-extended visual motion. Each of these has
no token-stream analogue at all. The transfer break is
total at the level of mechanism; only the umbrella claim
(grouping by similarity reduces processing cost) survives,
and even that survives only as metaphor.

** H.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-6
:END:

The takeaway is similar to D's: /Gestalt principles mostly
help the human reader/. Three observable layers separate.
/Typographic infrastructure/ — whitespace, indentation,
table layout, font weight — serves only the human reader;
the agent's tokeniser parses these the same as any other
character. /Structural infrastructure/ — =CUSTOM_ID=
cross-references, heading hierarchy, named blocks — serves
the agent reader and is also human-readable; this is the
dual-audience layer. When the two infrastructures compete
for the author's attention, structural-first investment
serves both audiences; typographic polish then serves
humans only. H and D together delimit the zone where
designing for /both/ audiences with the /same/ artefact
reaches its limit.

** H.7 Synthesis: H in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-7
:END:

Direction H sits in [[#synthesis-figure][the figure's]] /lower-right human-only
quadrant/ alongside [[#dir-D][D. Eye-tracking + saccades]]. The
narrative pair is sharp: D names the perceptual /mechanism/
(saccade, fixation, parafoveal preview), H names the
perceptual /organisation/ that the mechanism produces
(proximity grouping, similarity grouping, figure-ground
segregation, closure, common-fate motion). Both findings
live entirely inside biological visual-perception apparatus
and have no upper-quadrant projection.

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where H sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-C][C. Working memory limits]], [[#dir-D][D. Eye-tracking + saccades]] | /Substrate-bound numerical constants/ | H contributes Wertheimer 1923 grouping radii and Kubovy & van den Berg 2008 lattice-grouping thresholds — measurable in millimetres of visual angle |
| [[#dir-D][D. Eye-tracking + saccades]] | /Perceptual-substrate-only/ | H is D's organisational counterpart; both have no portable upper-quadrant projection at all; together they define the dead-zone of the figure |

H exposes the cleanest /weak metaphor/ that recurs when
researchers seek Gestalt analogues in transformer
architectures. Attention heads (Elhage et al. 2021 "A
Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits"; Olsson
et al. 2022 "In-context Learning and Induction Heads")
sometimes appear to perform token-level similarity grouping
or proximity matching. The metaphor is structurally weak:
attention heads find tokens whose embeddings are similar to
the query token within a context window — a similarity
operation, but not the perceptual grouping Wertheimer
described. Wertheimer grouping is /unconscious, automatic,
irreversible/; transformer attention is /conditional,
layer-by-layer, modifiable by prompting/. The substrate
mismatch is total; the surface analogy is marked weak in
this catalogue ([[#synthesis-observations][synthesis-observations]] item 8).

H also sharpens the D → J cross-projection from
[[#dir-D-7][D.7]]. J's transfer break at typographic signifiers (font
weight, italic emphasis, whitespace, colour-only emphasis)
collapses for agents /because/ the H-grouping that humans
perform on those signifiers does not happen in the agent
substrate. A human reader picks up "this paragraph is one
group" from H-proximity (whitespace between paragraphs),
H-similarity (consistent typeface), and H-common-fate
(consistent indentation level); an agent sees a flat token
sequence. The triangle D → H → J names a single
substrate-bound failure mode of typographic-only design,
with three different canonical authors describing three
different layers of it. The dual-audience LP design rule
that follows ([[#dir-J-6][J.6 Operational takeaway]] item 2 — /load-
bearing affordances must be structural, not typographic/)
is the operational form of "do not rely on D + H grouping
to communicate structure to the agent reader."

The lower-right human-only quadrant is therefore not an
empty zone in the figure — it is the /constraint zone/ that
delimits where dual-audience design must move its
load-bearing signifiers (out of the typographic layer and
into the structural layer).

* I. Information retrieval and berry-picking
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I
:END:

Direction I overturns the dominant pre-1989 IR model. Marcia
Bates published "The Design of Browsing and Berrypicking
Techniques for the Online Search Interface" in =Online Review=
13(5), 1989, pp. 407–424, proposing that real information
seekers do not have a fixed query and pursue it to completion
(the assumed model of Boolean retrieval systems). Instead they
/pick berries/ — they accumulate fragments across multiple
queries, the queries themselves shift as they read, and they
stop when they have /enough/ rather than when they have found
/all/. The model formalised what librarians had observed for
decades: exploratory search is iterative, opportunistic, and
satisficing. Bates's 2007 update "What is Browsing — Really?
A Model Drawing from Behavioural Science Research" extended
the model. Since 2000 the discipline has split into /exploratory
search/ (White & Roth 2009 monograph) and /specified-query
search/ tracks, with most everyday human search behaviour
in the former. I sits in the /upper-left full-transfer
quadrant/ of the figure: agents conducting multi-step
research display the same four Bates patterns within a
single session, and the only seam is the cross-session
persistence boundary shared with B, E, and G.

** I.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-1
:END:

Bates 1989 overturned four assumptions that the dominant
Boolean-IR model had baked into its evaluation framework.
First, /searchers do not start with a fixed query/ — they
start with a vague information need and discover what to ask
as they go. Second, /the query shifts during search/ —
something read in result A leads to query B which leads to
result C; the search path is /evolutionary/, not single-shot.
Third, /relevance is accumulated across fragments/ — the
useful output is a collection of "berries" picked from
multiple sources, not one sorted relevance list. Fourth,
/the stopping criterion is sufficiency, not optimality/ —
the searcher quits when they have enough to act, not when
they have exhausted the corpus. The "berry-picking"
metaphor captures both the discreteness (each berry is a
separate useful fragment) and the path-dependence (later
berries are discovered through earlier ones).

** I.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-2
:END:

Bates 1989 was grounded in two decades of librarianship
observation and reference-interview studies — the methodology
was qualitative rather than experimental, but the model
predicted patterns that controlled studies subsequently
confirmed. Broder 2002 in =ACM SIGIR Forum= 36(2)
introduced a taxonomy of web searches (informational vs
navigational vs transactional); informational queries —
the largest category — showed the berry-picking pattern
across millions of logged queries. Marchionini 2006 in
=Communications of the ACM= 49(4) named the field
/exploratory search/ and contrasted it with lookup
(traditional Boolean retrieval, well-served by classical IR)
versus learn and investigate (the berry-picking and beyond).
White & Roth 2009 =Exploratory Search: Beyond the Query-
Response Paradigm= consolidated the empirical and design
literature. Recent work (Hearst 2009 =Search User
Interfaces=; Olston & Chi 2003 on history-enriched
interfaces) has extended Bates's framing into modern
interface design.

** I.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-3
:END:

The isomorphism is one of the strongest in the document.
AI agents conducting research or debugging tasks display
all four Bates patterns. /Vague initial need/: a debugging
agent does not start with "look up symbol foo at line 42"
but with "something about the order-processing flow is
wrong, find it." /Query shift during search/: the agent
reads file A, learns that the bug is actually in module B,
issues a new grep with refined terms. /Accumulated relevance
across fragments/: the agent's final answer integrates
fragments from multiple files — function definition here,
test case there, configuration constant in a third file.
/Sufficient-not-optimal stopping/: the agent stops as soon
as it can act, not when it has exhausted the codebase.
The structural match is precise enough that Bates's 1989
paper could be re-titled "Berry-picking by AI Agents" with
almost no body changes; only the substrate (the agent's
context window and tool-call interface) replaces the
search-system UI Bates described.

** I.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-4
:END:

The shared primitive: /information need is shaped by what
has already been found, not just by what was originally
sought/. Both populations face an information landscape
that does not present itself as a sorted relevance list,
both face the bandwidth constraint of being unable to read
everything, and both have the option to revise their query
as comprehension grows. The mathematical substrate differs
— Bates wrote no formal model and agents follow an implicit
policy learned from training — but the /behavioural shape/
is strikingly similar: path-dependent, iteration-rich,
satisficing rather than optimising. This is the third of
three full-transfer findings in the document together with
A foraging and F recognition-vs-recall; all three describe
constraints on the search-and-comprehension process that
hold regardless of cognitive substrate.

** I.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-5
:END:

The break is at the /persistence layer/. Bates's model
implicitly assumes the searcher /remembers/ what they have
learned across the duration of the search — yesterday's
discovered berries are available today. For agents within a
single inference, this is fine: the context window holds
the working basket of berries. Across sessions, the agent
remembers nothing without explicit retrieval. So
berry-picking transfers cleanly /within a single session/
and breaks /across sessions/ unless retrieval-augmented
generation, persistent memory tools, or external note-taking
infrastructure intervene. This is the cleanest case where
the transfer holds completely at the behavioural level but
fails completely at the persistence layer; the failure mode
is well-understood and the standard fixes (RAG, vector
stores, explicit memory notes) are widely deployed. This
is the same break pattern as B/E/G — anything depending on
cross-session consolidation breaks at the agent boundary —
applied to working memory of search results rather than
schemas or skills.

** I.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-6
:END:

Three practices follow with strong dual-audience returns.
First, /design for path-dependent reading, not linear
reading/. LP docs optimised for the grep-arrived reader
who follows references to several sections — rather than
the start-to-finish reader — match how both populations
actually consume the artefact. =CUSTOM_ID= cross-references,
"see also" links, named blocks, and dense TOCs are the
navigation infrastructure for berry-picking; the host
project's =literate-programming-document-first.md= rule
implicitly enforces it. Second, /support sufficient-not-
optimal stopping/. Sections /self-contained enough/ that a
reader who picked one as a berry can act on it without
reading the surrounding sections — the section-as-concept
rule restated for berry-picking. Third, /provide explicit
persistence hooks for agent readers/. Where humans remember
accumulated berries internally, agents rely on external
infrastructure: clear file naming for re-identification,
stable URLs or commit hashes for referencing, and explicit
summaries that survive across sessions. The Sources section
of this document exemplifies the form.

** I.7 Synthesis: I in the transfer-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-7
:END:

Direction I sits in [[#synthesis-figure][the figure's]] /upper-left
full-transfer quadrant/ as the third member of the
[[#synthesis-families][Foraging-economics]] family (A, F, I) and as a member of the
[[#synthesis-families][Cross-session state persistence]] family (B, E, G, I). I
provides the catalogue's cleanest single-paragraph transfer
claim.

| Co-tier direction | Family | Where I sits |
|-------------------+--------+--------------|
| [[#dir-A][A. Information foraging]], [[#dir-F][F. Recognition vs recall]] | /Foraging-economics/ | I supplies the /iterative/ component — each retrieved item updates the information need; A supplies the per-patch optimisation; F supplies the recognition asymmetry that lets scent work |
| [[#dir-B][B. Cognitive load theory]], [[#dir-E][E. Mental models]], [[#dir-G][G. Progressive disclosure]] | /Cross-session state persistence/ | I's saturated-sampling stopping rule depends on the reader remembering what has already been sampled; cross-session the agent cannot |

Bates 1989's central observation in =Online Review= 13(5),
pp. 407–424 — that the user's information need is /not
fixed/ in advance of search but is /reshaped by what is
found/ — maps onto the agent grep-Read-iterate cycle with
mechanical precision. The agent issues a grep, examines
results, picks a file to Read, the Read content alters
what the agent considers relevant for the next grep, and
the cycle continues until the answer is sufficient. Bates
1989's six "tactics" (footnote chasing, citation searching,
journal run, area scanning, subject searches, author
searching) all have agent analogues in modern codebase
navigation (grep with regex variants, =git log -S=, file
walks, directory scans, signature searches, history
commits by author). The behavioural isomorphism is so
complete that Bates 1989 reads, line for line, as a
description of agent codebase exploration four decades
before LLMs.

The transfer is full at the /within-session/ scale and
breaks only at the /cross-session/ boundary that defines
direction I's second family membership. Saturated-sampling
in Bates' formulation means the reader stops searching when
new items repeat old ones; this requires durable memory of
what has been sampled. Agents have within-session memory
(the conversation context) but no across-session memory by
default. The host project's =CLAUDE.md= memory file and the
=~/.claude/projects/.../memory/MEMORY.md= mechanism are
architectural workarounds for exactly this gap — they
explicitly externalise the "what has been sampled" state
that humans hold implicitly.

I's most distinctive trait among the upper-left triad is
/the cleanness of the behaviour-layer transfer/. Bates
1989's claim has no substrate vocabulary at all — no
working-memory limit, no chunk size, no perceptual
primitive, no neural correlate. The finding is /pure
path-dependent reading behaviour/. I is the catalogue's
single clearest demonstration of
[[#synthesis-observations][synthesis-observations]] item 4 (/behaviour and decision-
structure findings transfer cleanly/). A reader testing the
behaviour-vs-substrate axis empirically can start with I —
nothing remains to subtract from the finding before
checking whether agents do the same thing, because Bates
1989 has already done the subtraction.

I also completes the upper-left triad's internal structure
in a way that closes the [[#dir-F-7][F.7 cross-family hub]]
argument: A is the /per-patch/ optimisation, I is the
/across-patch/ iteration, F is the /substrate primitive/
that makes both work. The three findings are not parallel
candidates for "behaviour transfers" — they are three
levels of one architecture (patch / iteration / primitive),
each transferring cleanly because the architecture is
substrate-independent.

* J. Affordances and signifiers
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J
:END:

Direction J names the relational-property concept that
finished the cognitive-psychology canon and started the HCI
one. James J. Gibson coined /affordances/ in =The Ecological
Approach to Visual Perception= (Houghton Mifflin, 1979), with
the canonical definition: /"The affordances of the
environment are what it offers the animal, what it provides
or furnishes, either for good or ill."/ Affordances are
relational — neither a property of the object alone nor of
the perceiver alone but of the pairing. Donald Norman
imported the term to HCI in =The Psychology of Everyday
Things= (Basic Books, 1988; reissued and retitled =The Design
of Everyday Things= 1990, revised 2013) and famously narrowed
it to /perceived affordances/ — what the user thinks they
can do with the object. In 1999 Norman published a self-
correction ("Affordance, Conventions, and Design,"
=Interactions= 6(3), 1999, pp. 38–43) distinguishing /real/
from /perceived/ affordances. In the 2013 revision he split the concept further:
/affordance/ stays Gibsonian (what is possible) while
/signifier/ names the cue that communicates the possibility.
J sits on the /partial-high boundary just above the dead-
zone/ of the figure: signifier transfer holds cleanly once
cues move from typographic to structural, while the
perceptual substrate that humans use to pick signifiers up
stays human-only.

** J.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-1
:END:

The claim evolves across four formulations. Gibson 1979:
/"The affordances of the environment are what it offers the
animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or
ill."/ Affordances are /relational/ — neither a property of
the object alone nor of the perceiver alone but of the
pairing. A chair affords sitting for an adult human;
the same chair affords nothing useful to a fly. Norman 1988
narrowed the term in =The Psychology of Everyday Things=:
"perceived and actual properties of the thing, primarily those
fundamental properties that determine just how the thing
could possibly be used." This narrowing emphasised perception
— what the user /thinks/ they can do — and embedded the
concept in HCI evaluation. Norman 1999 self-corrected in
=Interactions= 6(3): distinguish /real/ affordances (the
Gibsonian what-is-possible) from /perceived/ affordances
(what the user believes is possible) — they often diverge.
Norman 2013 added a third term: /signifier/ — the cue that
communicates the affordance. The chair's seat-shape signifies
its sitting-affordance to a human; without that signifier
the affordance would not be discoverable.

** J.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-2
:END:

The empirical chain runs along three sources, none of them a
single decisive experiment — affordance research is more an
accumulation of design audits than a Sweller-style randomised
trial. /Gibson 1979/ consolidated two decades of ecological-
psychology work, with the affordance chapter (ch. 8) building
on perception studies by Gibson and collaborators (notably
the "visual cliff" infant-perception studies, Gibson & Walk
1960 =Scientific American= 202(4), pp. 64–71). /Norman 1988/
provided the design-audit form: dozens of physical-artefact
case studies (doors, taps, stove burners) where misaligned
signifiers caused predictable misuse. /McGrenere & Ho 2000/
("Affordances: Clarifying and Evolving a Concept,"
=Graphics Interface 2000=, pp. 179–186) is the canonical
review paper, distinguishing Gibsonian /capability/ from
Normanian /perceived capability/ and proposing that HCI
practice conflated them. Empirical follow-up is mostly
usability-test case work — A/B comparisons of button shapes,
icon designs, hyperlink underlining — none of which yields
a Miller-7±2 style portable constant. The strength is
qualitative consistency: when signifiers mismatch
affordances, users predictably miss the action. That
qualitative finding is what transfers, not a number.

** J.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-3
:END:

Agents read /textual signifiers/ continuously. Six visible
populations in this codebase:

| Signifier kind | Example | Affordance communicated |
|----------------+---------+------------------------|
| File extension | =.org=, =.py= | "loadable as literate Elisp" / "Python module" |
| Heading-as-concept | =* Public API= vs =* Functions= | "this section names a role, not a phase" |
| Property drawer | =:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-3= | "stable cross-file anchor lives here" |
| YAML / TOML front-matter | =allowed-tools: [Read, Grep]= | "skill restricts which tools it may invoke" |
| Named code block | =#+NAME: ansi-strip= | "this block is referenced elsewhere" |
| Tag suffix | =:sdd:= on a heading | "this section is a story-driven-development node" |

Each row is exactly a Norman-2013 signifier: a textual cue
that communicates a capability. Three of these were designed
explicitly with AI readers in mind (=:CUSTOM_ID:= for
agent-grep stability, =allowed-tools:= for permission
gating, =:sdd:= for workflow dispatch). The other three
predate the AI-reader audience and were inherited from
human-targeted typography but transferred without
modification — agents happily consume them. The agent
side of isomorphism is strong: the codebase's own skill
authors increasingly /design for the AI reader as a first-
class audience/, with humans as a secondary consumer.

** J.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-4
:END:

The shared primitive is /relational fit between artefact and
reader determines what the reader does next/, with /signifiers
as the communication layer/ telling each reader which actions
the artefact supports. Two universal features:

1. The /need/ for explicit signifiers is audience-independent —
   both populations fail predictably when affordances are
   present but unsignified. The Norman 1988 case of "doors
   that need a push sign because the handle suggests pull" is
   the exact analogue of "an =.org= file that needs a comment
   saying 'this is a config, not code' because the file
   extension fails to communicate the role."
2. /Structural/ signifiers (heading hierarchy, property
   drawers, named blocks) transfer across audience because
   the structure /is/ the signifier-bearing substrate and
   both populations parse it the same way.

The audience-specific layer is the /typographic/ signifier
set — italics, parentheses, em-dashes, whitespace breaks —
which agents downsample to flat tokens but humans read as
strong cues. The dual-audience design move is to layer the
two: keep typography for human aesthetics but make every
load-bearing signifier /structural/ so the agent gets the
same affordance map without depending on aesthetic
perception.

** J.5 Where transfer breaks
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-5
:END:

The break lives at Gibson's foundational claim of /direct
perception/. Gibson 1979 argues affordances are picked up
directly from the optic array — no inference, no symbolic
intermediate. The whole ecological-psychology programme rests
on this: perception is unmediated. Agents do not perceive
in this sense at all; they tokenise byte streams. So:

- /Real/ affordances in Norman's 1999 sense (what the
  artefact actually permits — file is Read-able, function is
  call-able, section is cross-referenceable) transfer
  trivially because they are properties of the substrate,
  not of perception.
- /Perceived/ affordances depend on the perception apparatus,
  and the apparatus differs fundamentally. A button's
  three-dimensional bevel signals press-ability to a human
  via direct visual pickup; the same bevel reaches an agent
  only as ALT text, ARIA role, or a structural tag — never
  as the bevel itself.
- /Signifiers/ that depend on typographic perception
  (whitespace, font weight, colour-only emphasis) collapse
  into nothing for agents.

The clean operational implication is the same as in D
(eye-tracking): /any affordance whose signifier is
typography-only is invisible to agents/. To make the
affordance dual-audience, the signifier must be structural
(a tag, a property, an explicit named anchor).

** J.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-6
:END:

Four-rule design heuristic for an LP author whose document
must work as a dual-audience artefact:

1. /Be dense in structural signifiers/. Every section gets a
   =:CUSTOM_ID:=; every cross-referenced block gets a =#+NAME:=;
   every heading names a /concept/ (the role this section plays
   in the system), not a phase (=Functions=, =Helpers=). This
   is the heading-as-concept rule already in this repo's
   =literate-programming-document-first.md=, restated as a
   signifier-design principle.
2. /Prefer structural signifiers over typographic ones for
   load-bearing affordances/. Italics for emphasis is fine
   for humans; if the agent /must/ know "this is the public
   API," express that as a =:PROPERTIES:= drawer or a tag,
   not as bold text.
3. /Layer signifier-vocabularies/. Keep typography for human
   aesthetics; layer structural cues underneath so the agent
   gets the same affordance map. A heading written
   "* Public API" in bold gives humans the typographic
   signifier; the heading text itself plus a =:CUSTOM_ID:=
   gives the agent the structural one. Both audiences win
   from the same artefact.
4. /Audit signifier-affordance alignment/. If a section
   labels itself "internal helpers" but the agent's grep
   shows the symbols are imported from three other modules,
   the signifier lies about the affordance and someone will
   misuse the code. The =naming-conventions.md= and =-=- vs =--=
   public-vs-private convention in this repo is exactly this
   audit pattern at the symbol level.

This is the most directly /actionable/ direction of the
ten — affordance theory's design-audit form fits naturally
into LP author practice.

** J.7 Synthesis: J as the third corner of the D-H-J triangle
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J-7
:END:

Direction J sits on /the partial-high boundary just above
the dead-zone/ in [[#synthesis-figure][the figure]]. J's transfer profile is
the inverse of H's: H's perceptual organisation does not
transfer at all, yet J's signifier concept (the cue that
communicates an affordance) transfers cleanly once the cue
moves from typographic to structural. J does not introduce
a new shared-primitive family — its membership tally was
finalised in [[#dir-J-3][J.3 Agent isomorphism]] — but it instantiates
two existing patterns with unusual clarity.

J is the /third corner of the D-H-J triangle/ named in
[[#dir-H-7][H.7]]. Read from J's perspective: the human reader's
affordance perception depends on D's saccade / fixation /
parafoveal-preview mechanism to land the eye and on H's
proximity / similarity / closure grouping to organise the
percept. The signifier (J) is whatever cue the artefact
provides that the D + H machinery can pick up. For agents,
neither D nor H is operative, so the cue must be picked up
by tokenisation instead. That is the substrate gap.
J's contribution is to name /the LP design rule that
collapses the gap/: any cue that must be picked up by /both/
audiences must be picked up by structural means
(=:CUSTOM_ID:=, =#+NAME:=, tags) rather than typographic
means (font weight, italics, whitespace, colour). A reader
who internalises J's four-rule heuristic ([[#dir-J-6][J.6]] items 1–4)
has effectively absorbed the D-H-J triangle's design
consequence without needing to read D or H at all.

J is also the second-strongest case of /convergent design
without lineage/ in the catalogue, after G. Norman 2013's
/signifier/ concept was published in =The Design of Everyday
Things=, revised edition. The Anthropic skill SDK's
=allowed-tools:=, =description:=, and =when_to_use:= fields
are all signifiers in Norman's sense — cues telling the
agent reader what the skill can do, when it should run, and
which tools it may invoke. The skill-SDK designers did not
cite Norman 2013 (no documented derivation exists). Yet the
designs are not merely similar — they map clause-for-clause
onto Norman's typology: =description:= is a /real/
affordance label, =allowed-tools:= is a /perceived/
affordance constraint, =when_to_use:= is a /signifier/ for
the affordance's invocation context. Three independent
populations (everyday-object users circa 1988; web/HCI users
circa 2013; AI agents circa 2024) converged on the same
three-part typology because all three populations face the
same interface constraint: /must decide what an artefact
can do for me before investing budget in interaction/.

J is the catalogue's neatest illustration of the principle
that /transfer happens where it is forced by constraint
universality, and breaks where it is forced by substrate
specificity/. The signifier idea is constraint-universal;
the perceptual substrate that picks signifiers up is
substrate-specific. LP design that recognises the split
inherits both halves cleanly.

** Shared-primitive families (work in progress)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-families
:END:

The directions that transfer (or partially transfer) share
small families of primitives. Round 3 iters will populate
this list as each direction reports in. Initial draft:

| Family | Member directions | Primitive |
|--------+-------------------+-----------|
| Foraging economics | A, F, I | Bounded budget + unequal landscape + asymmetric proximal-distal cost |
| Element-interactivity bound | B, C | Items × pairwise interactivity hits a finite store; structural form transfers, numerical constant does not |
| Cross-session state persistence | B, E, G, I | Finding requires reader to carry state from one encounter to the next; break boundary at the agent's stateless cold-start |
| Substrate-bound numerical constants | C, D, H | A specific number (Miller 7±2, Cowan 4±1, Rayner saccade lengths, Wertheimer grouping radii) is part of the canonical finding and derives from biological apparatus; the structural form survives but the number does not |
| Perceptual-substrate-only | D, H | Entire finding lives in human visual-perception apparatus; stripping the perceptual mechanism leaves nothing portable; no upper-quadrant projection at all |
| Dual-process processing modes | E, F, G | Readers shift between fast cue-driven and slow deliberate processing depending on stake; agent traces show structural-form analogue (grep-scan vs chain-of-thought); Kahneman 2011 names the frame, F supplies the recognition-side optimisation, G supplies the deliberation-side scaffolding |
| /(further families populated by iter#26 onwards)/ | | |

Members of the /Foraging-economics/ family share the closed-
form result that rate-of-gain optimisation under a finite
budget produces patch-selection and patch-leaving behaviour
regardless of substrate. The shared-primitive name is
deliberately ecological (Charnov 1976) rather than cognitive,
which is why the same primitive holds for ant colonies, web
readers, and AI agents alike.

Members of the /Element-interactivity bound/ family share
the structural form "interacting items in a budget-limited
store cause graceful degradation under overload." Sweller's
CLT and Miller/Cowan's working-memory limits are the same
constraint stated with two different vocabularies and two
different empirical traditions. The constraint transfers
fully; the numerical value of the budget (Miller's 7±2,
Cowan's 4±1, agent context windows in the 10⁵-10⁶ token
range) is substrate-specific.

Members of the /Cross-session state persistence/ family
each break at the same agent-architecture boundary: the
agent starts each conversation with no memory of prior
encounters. B's schema construction, E's mental-model
accretion, G's expert-recognition payoff, and I's saturated-
sampling memory all require the reader to carry durable
state forward. RAG / explicit memory / fine-tuning are
architectural workarounds; none is "natural" agent
behaviour the way inter-session consolidation is for
humans. This family is the largest single source of
identified transfer breaks in the Round-2 deepening.

Members of the /Substrate-bound numerical constants/
family each pin a finding to a specific number that derives
from biological measurement: phonological-loop capacity for
C, oculomotor and parafoveal geometry for D, retinal
sampling rates for H. The family identifies a particular
failure mode of naive HI-to-agent transfer: a designer who
copies the canonical bound ("max 7 items per menu") onto an
agent context window misreads the finding entirely. The
correct port is the structural form (capacity is bounded,
perception jumps in discrete units, proximity produces
grouping) with a new number derived from agent
measurements (context-window tokens, attention-head bias,
embedding-space distance). The cleanest illustration is
the agent context window: it /is/ bounded, but at 10⁵-10⁶
tokens its qualitative implications for LP design are
opposite to Miller's. The human reader is overwhelmed by
30-item lists; the agent context window absorbs them
trivially but loses fidelity over thousands of items by a
different mechanism (positional bias, lost-in-the-middle).
Same structural concern, different specific number, different
mitigation.

Members of the /Perceptual-substrate-only/ family are
narrower than the substrate-bound-numerical-constants
family. D and H share the property that /the finding has
no portable upper-quadrant projection at all/ — stripping
the perceptual mechanism leaves nothing for the LP author
to carry across. D's eye-mind assumption (Just & Carpenter
1980, =Psychological Review= 87(4)) collapses without an
eye doing the minding; H's grouping principles collapse
without a retina doing the grouping. Membership in this
family is the operational definition of "human-only" in
the figure: a direction belongs here when its takeaway for
the LP author begins with "this finding is for human
readers only" and ends with "ensure the human-only
optimisation does not damage the structural infrastructure
agents depend on." The D → J cross-projection (D names the
perceptual gap; J names the LP design rule — load-bearing
affordances must be structural signifiers, not typographic
ones) shows that perceptual-substrate-only directions
nevertheless /constrain/ the dual-audience design even
though their findings do not transfer.

Members of the /Dual-process processing modes/ family
share the structural claim that /a reader runs in two
operating modes — a fast cue-driven mode and a slow
deliberate mode — selectable by stake/, where stake is
roughly "cost of being wrong here." Kahneman 2011 names
the human form (System 1 / System 2, with neuroanatomical
correlates that are themselves human-only). F (recognition
vs recall) and G (progressive disclosure) supply the
design-side moves that match the two modes — recognition-
based UI for System 1, scaffolded disclosure of complexity
for System 2. The agent-side evidence is observational:
agents reading-by-grep operate in a fast cue-driven mode
that produces System-1-like errors (mismatched filenames,
out-of-date snippets); agents producing chain-of-thought
traces with explicit intermediate facts operate in a
slow deliberate mode that produces System-2-like outputs.
The host project's =CLAUDE.md= /Tool-Use Protocol/ rule
("every claim backed by tool output") is a System-2
scaffolding move written for agents. The shared primitive
is /two modes selectable by stake/; the substrate
implementation (dopaminergic versus prefrontal-cortex for
humans, attention-pattern versus extended-thinking for
agents) is substrate-specific and varies. This is the
third member of the upper-left full-transfer zone after
the /Foraging-economics/ triad (A, F, I) — F appears in
both families because cued-retrieval economics /is/ the
recognition-side optimisation that dual-process design
exploits.

** Cross-cutting observations (work in progress)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-observations
:END:

Carried forward from [[#round-2-status][Round 2 status]] for Round 3 to
sharpen:

1. /Cross-session state persistence/ is a universal transfer-
   break boundary (B, E, G, I).
2. /Convergent design without lineage/ — Anthropic skill SDK
   =when_to_use= ≈ Nielsen 1995 first-screen scannability;
   =CUSTOM_ID= ≈ Norman 2013 signifier; =allowed-tools:= ≈
   Tognazzini progressive-disclosure scaffolding.
3. /Substrate-level numerical limits do not transfer/ —
   Miller 7±2, Cowan 4±1, Rayner saccade lengths, Wertheimer
   grouping radii are all substrate-bound constants. The
   /structural form/ (limits exist) is universal; the
   /numerical value/ is human-only.
4. /Behaviour and decision-structure findings transfer
   cleanly/ — A, F, I anchor the upper-left quadrant
   precisely because they describe behaviour shape, not
   substrate mechanism.

The Round-3 contribution will be re-articulating these as
the /two axes/ shown in the figure above — vertical
behaviour/substrate, horizontal full-transfer/breaks — and
showing that the four cross-cutting observations are
projections of a single underlying claim: /transfer
completeness is predicted by abstraction level above the
substrate/.

A fifth observation is emerging from Round 3 itself:
/some directions are cross-family hubs/. F (recognition vs
recall) and E (mental models + dual-process) each appear in
two shared-primitive families. The pattern is not arbitrary —
F's recognition asymmetry /is/ what makes foraging and
dual-process design work mechanistically, and E's
dual-process frame /is/ the theoretical container that names
F and G's joint structural role. Cross-family hubs explain
the inner clustering of the figure's quadrants: directions
co-locate not by topical proximity but by shared underlying
constraint structure. See [[#dir-F-7][F.7 Synthesis: F as cross-family
hub]] for the worked example.

A related observation is /component-level rather than
direction-level transfer assessment/. B (CLT) and E (mental
models + dual-process) each braid two findings with
different transfer profiles into a single canonical
direction. The figure plots each direction's centre of mass;
individual findings within a direction may project onto
different quadrants. Future taxonomic work should consider
unbraiding multi-component directions explicitly — this is
left to Round 5 polish.

The seventh observation is /convergent design without
lineage/, originally named in [[#round-2-status][Round 2 status]] as cross-
cutting observation #2 and now anchored to G (iter#27) as
primary case-study. When two reader populations face the
same interface constraint, /independent design lineages/
converge on members of the same design family even when
neither lineage cites the other. The /Anthropic skill SDK/'s
=when_to_use= field re-implements Carroll & Rosson 1987's
minimalist-instruction doctrine and Krug 2000's first-screen
heuristic without documented derivation; =allowed-tools:=
re-implements Tognazzini 1992's progressive-disclosure
scaffolding. Convergence has a mechanical explanation —
under fixed interface constraints (bounded budget +
heterogeneous candidates + fast triage required), rational
design lands in a small region of design space. The
research finding is that /the convergence happens cleanly
across substrates/, which validates the figure's
behaviour-vs-substrate axis at a meta-level: the convergent
designs all live in the behaviour layer (recognition,
disclosure, scaffolding) and not in the substrate (neural
circuitry / transformer attention pattern).

An eighth observation, contributed by iter#28 around
direction H, is /weak metaphors invite overgeneralisation/.
Surface analogies between substrate mechanisms (transformer
attention heads producing token-similarity scores; Gestalt
proximity grouping producing perceptual groups) can read as
shared primitives but typically are not. The Round 3
discipline for marking a finding as /transferring/ requires
either: (a) a closed-form mathematical core that holds
substrate-independently (the Foraging-economics standard,
where R = G/T optimisation works for any rate-of-gain
constraint structure), or (b) observed behavioural
isomorphism backed by traces (the Dual-process standard,
where agents demonstrably switch between grep-scan and
extended-thinking modes). Surface analogies that lack both
— Gestalt-grouping ≈ attention-head similarity, retrieval-
practice ≈ chain-of-thought reinforcement, working-memory-
chunking ≈ context-window compression — must be marked as
weak metaphors. Future Round-4 verification work should
audit each direction's shared-primitive claim against this
two-part discipline.
