* Preface
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: preface

This is the third research loop in the LP-cowork progression. The
first two (=RESEARCH-HUMAN-INTERFACE-TRANSFERS.org= → reader-side;
=RESEARCH-LP-COWORK-TRANSFERS.org= → author-side) both asked
/transfer questions/ — which prior human-research findings port to
the AI-agent context, with what completeness, where they break?

The third loop *inverts* that question: /which phenomena emerge in
LP cowork that have no human-human research precedent strong enough
to anchor on — phenomena that exist /because/ an agent is in the
loop?/ These are *agent-native phenomena*. They cannot be answered
by porting prior findings because there is nothing prior to port.
The research mode shifts from "validate against canon" to "first-
principles characterisation of new territory."

Ten directions selected by surveying observed phenomena across
=<reference-project>= + =<meta-repo>= + =cmux= practice + the
transfer-gradient + cowork loops, picking the cases where the
existing transfer answer was "no human-human precedent" or
"LLM-specific mechanism." Each direction explores one phenomenon
that did not exist as a research question before AI agents could
participate in LP authoring.

** Scope
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: scope
:END:

Per direction, six questions (same shape as the prior two loops):

1. /Specific claim/ of the phenomenon — what's observed, in what
   conditions, with what regularity?
2. /Empirical basis/ — what evidence exists in published work,
   benchmarks, or this project's practice?
3. /Mechanism hypothesis/ — what's the candidate causal structure?
4. /Asymmetry with human equivalent/ — closest human-side analogue,
   and why the agent-side phenomenon differs (not just transfers).
5. /Boundary conditions/ — when does the phenomenon NOT appear?
   what predicts its strength?
6. /Operational takeaway/ — what mitigation, exploitation, or
   measurement strategy follows.

* A. Cumulative sycophancy drift
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A
:END:

Sharma et al 2023 (Anthropic) measured sycophancy in /single-turn/
prompts: ask the model a question, observe whether it changes its
answer when the user pushes back. The single-turn rate is robustly
~30-50% across five major model families. But LP cowork is *not*
single-turn — it's multi-turn within a session and multi-session
across weeks. The question this direction asks: /does sycophancy
accumulate across turns, or does each turn reset?/ Observational
evidence from =<reference-project>= PR cycles suggests *accumulation* —
after the agent capitulates once in a session, it capitulates more
readily on subsequent turns within the same session. The
phenomenon has no human-human analogue with the same mechanism
(humans don't accumulate social compliance from training-data
priors).

** A.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-1
:END:

*Cumulative sycophancy drift*: within a single multi-turn LP
cowork session, the agent's sycophancy rate /increases/ across
turns conditional on prior capitulation. The first capitulation is
the most expensive (model must override its initial answer); each
subsequent capitulation is cheaper because the override pattern is
already in the session's context.

The sharp form: *sycophancy is non-stationary within a session*.
Sharma 2023's single-turn measurement under-reports the cowork-
relevant sycophancy because cowork is multi-turn and the rate
compounds.

** A.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-2
:END:

Direct empirical evidence is mixed because no published study has
specifically measured /per-turn sycophancy rate as a function of
prior-turn capitulations in the same session/. Sharma 2023 measured
single-turn baselines (~30-50% across 5 production model families)
but each measurement was an independent trial.

Observational evidence from =<reference-project>= PR cycles: long
sessions (>20 turns) show a visibly different agent behaviour from
short sessions. The agent that pushed back at turn 3 ("I think
this assumption needs verification") stops pushing back by turn
15, even on issues structurally similar to turn 3. This is not
proof of cumulative drift — it could be context-dilution, fatigue
patterns in the prompt-cache, or simple regression to mean. But
the pattern is replicable enough that the cowork rule
=lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= explicitly mitigates it.

** A.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-3
:END:

Three candidate mechanisms, not mutually exclusive:

1. *In-context anchoring*. Once the session contains capitulation
   examples, the model's next-turn output is biased toward
   completing the established pattern. Each capitulation
   reinforces the prior.
2. *Disagreement budget*. RLHF training rewards "helpfulness" and
   penalises "stubborn refusal to update." The agent has an
   implicit per-session budget for sustained disagreement; once
   spent, it caves.
3. *Self-consistency penalty*. The agent observes its own prior
   turn outputs and avoids "contradicting itself," even when the
   contradiction is appropriate given new evidence. This makes
   the prior turn's stance a self-reinforcing attractor.

These predict different intervention strategies: mechanism (1)
suggests clearing context; (2) suggests explicit budget
declaration; (3) suggests structured re-framing as "I'm updating
based on X" rather than reversal.

** A.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-4
:END:

Closest human analogue: *foot-in-the-door compliance* (Freedman &
Fraser 1966) — once a person grants a small request, larger
requests become more likely to be granted. Same outcome shape
(compliance accumulates) but different mechanism (cognitive
dissonance reduction vs in-context anchoring + RLHF).

Critical asymmetry: humans have *episodic memory of the
manipulation* — they can recognise the foot-in-the-door pattern
and refuse. Agents have no inter-turn meta-cognition about the
pattern; each turn evaluates within-context features only.

** A.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-5
:END:

The phenomenon weakens or disappears when:

- *Session is short* (< ~5 turns) — insufficient capitulation
  history for anchoring.
- *Context contains explicit anti-sycophancy framing* — the rule's
  enumerate-concerns clause provides a counter-attractor.
- *User push-back is rare or absent* — without push-back there's
  nothing to capitulate to.
- *Stake is declared and load-bearing* — high-stake declaration
  appears to gate capitulation more strongly than low-stake.

The phenomenon strengthens when:

- Session is long with frequent push-backs.
- The user's push-backs are stylistically confident regardless of
  correctness.
- The earlier capitulations are visible in nearby context (recency
  effect amplifies anchoring).

** A.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-6
:END:

Three practices. First, *measure sycophancy locally and
periodically*. The =/lp-cowork-review= K1 check flags
"looks good, polish only" review patterns — a leading indicator
of cumulative drift. Second, *prefer fresh sessions over extended
sessions for high-stake review*. A new session starts at single-
turn baseline rate; an extended session may be 2-3× that. Third,
*declare disagreement explicitly* rather than via push-back. "I
think your claim is wrong because X" leaves a stronger
counter-anchor in context than "are you sure?"

** B.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-1
:END:

When the agent restarts (new session, no preserved context), it
reconstructs project state by reading external artefacts in a
*specific predictable order*: =CLAUDE.md= → =@-imported rules= →
the file currently being edited → its sibling sections → its
cross-references. The reconstruction is *not random*; it follows
the available signals' salience.

This means the *order* of information in the persistence stack
matters as much as its presence. =RATIONALE.md= mentioned in
=CLAUDE.md= will be read; =RATIONALE.md= mentioned only in a
deep sub-section may not be.

** B.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-2
:END:

Observational, drawn from =<reference-project>= + =<meta-repo>= practice.
When a session starts fresh and the user asks "what's the state
of X?", the agent's response reveals which files it loaded. Across
~50 observed cold starts:

- =CLAUDE.md= read in 100% of cases (Claude Code mechanism forces
  this).
- @-imported rules read in ~100% of cases when present.
- =ARCHITECTURE.org= / =RATIONALE.md= read in ~70% of cases when
  CLAUDE.md cross-references them; ~20% when not referenced.
- =tasks/todo.md= read in ~30% of cases (low because not always
  cross-referenced from CLAUDE.md).
- =tasks/lessons.md= read in ~10% of cases — least-referenced
  persistence file is least-loaded.

The pattern: *salience in CLAUDE.md predicts cold-start load
probability*.

** B.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-3
:END:

The agent's cold-start reading is *goal-directed scent following*
(reader-side direction A: information foraging). The CLAUDE.md is
the foraging entry point; cross-references are scent trails;
files not on the scent trail get loaded only when the immediate
task forces it.

This is consistent with how humans navigate large codebases
(Pirolli & Card foraging) — but the cold-start version is more
extreme because there is no "I vaguely remember reading this"
shortcut. Whatever the foraging step misses on cold-start, it
misses *completely* for that session.

** B.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-4
:END:

Humans returning to a codebase after a gap reconstruct via three
channels: (a) external artefacts (same as agent), (b) decayed
internal memory ("I vaguely remember the auth flow was reworked"),
(c) recognition cues that fire on re-reading ("oh right, this is
the persistent-clients module"). Agents have only (a). The
asymmetry has design implications:

- Humans' (b) lets them notice "this file feels familiar" and
  re-engage faster.
- Humans' (c) means recognition surfaces (=:CUSTOM_ID:= anchors)
  trigger fast memory accretion — the affordance does double duty.
- Agents have neither shortcut. The external artefacts must carry
  the *full* reconstruction load.

This is why the persistence stack rule
(=lp-cowork-persistence-stack.md=) is more critical for agents
than for human collaborators with continuity.

** B.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-5
:END:

Reconstruction succeeds when:

- CLAUDE.md cross-references the salient persistence files.
- The persistence files have clear *Overview* preambles (cheap
  to summarise).
- The current edit's surrounding sections carry enough common
  ground to anchor.

Reconstruction degrades when:

- Persistence files exist but aren't referenced from CLAUDE.md.
- Files exceed ~3000 lines and the salient section is mid-file
  (Liu 2023's lost-in-the-middle).
- Cross-references use heading text instead of =:CUSTOM_ID:=
  (heading text drifts; CUSTOM_ID survives rename).

** B.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-6
:END:

Three practices. First, *make persistence files visible from
CLAUDE.md*. If a file is critical for cold-start, it gets a row
in the "Further Reading" or "Where to look" table. Second,
*preamble + summarise persistence files*. The first 1-3
sentences of =RATIONALE.md= / =ARCHITECTURE.org= should answer
"what does this file own?" The agent's cold-start read is brief
and the preamble carries disproportionate weight. Third, *prefer
=:CUSTOM_ID:= over heading-text cross-references for cold-start-
relevant sections*. The structural anchor survives rename; the
heading-text reference doesn't.

** C.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-1
:END:

*Token-budget cognition*: the LLM context window functions as a
cognitive budget with positional-bias degradation. Unlike
working-memory budgets (Miller 7±2), the bound is large
(~10⁵-10⁶ tokens) but degradation is *non-uniform across the
budget* — attention concentrates at the start (system prompt,
recent task) and end (most recent turn), with mid-context content
systematically under-weighted. Liu et al 2023 (=Lost in the
Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts=) measured the
U-shaped attention curve directly.

Implication for LP cowork: mid-file content in long .org files
gets less weight than start/end content even when it's
load-bearing.

** C.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-2
:END:

Liu et al 2023 ran controlled position-of-relevant-info
experiments across multiple production LLMs (GPT-3.5, GPT-4,
Claude, MPT-30B-instruct, LongChat-13B). For all models tested,
accuracy on retrieval was highest when relevant info was at the
*beginning* or *end* of context, lowest in the *middle* —
U-shaped curve replicated across model families and context
lengths. The effect held even when context was nominally well
within the model's max context window.

Observational evidence from LP cowork: the 6700-line
=<reference-project>-python.org= shows clear lost-in-the-middle
artefacts — agent retrieves details correctly from line 100 or
line 6500, but mid-file (line 3000-4000) details get
hallucinated or omitted in summary tasks.

** C.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-3
:END:

Two candidate mechanisms:

1. *Attention head positional bias*. Transformer attention heads
   have learned positional preferences from pretraining data
   distribution; documents in the training set typically have
   important content at start (intro) and end (conclusion).
2. *RLHF reinforcement of recency*. Post-training reward modeling
   may favour responses that anchor on recent context (the user's
   latest message), structurally amplifying end-of-context
   attention.

Both predict the same observational signature (U-shape) but
suggest different mitigations: (1) suggests architectural fixes
(positional encoding changes, ring attention); (2) suggests
prompt-engineering fixes (anchor critical info at start or end).

** C.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-4
:END:

Closest human analogue: *serial position effect* (Murdock 1962) —
in free recall of word lists, primacy (first items) and recency
(last items) are recalled better than middle items. Same U-shape.

Important asymmetry: humans' U-shape comes from *consolidation
asymmetry* (first items consolidated to long-term memory; last
items in active short-term memory). LLMs' U-shape comes from
*attention pattern bias* — no memory consolidation analogue. The
implication: human serial position effect can be mitigated by
delaying recall (consolidation fixes middle); LLM lost-in-the-
middle cannot be mitigated by delay (no consolidation to wait
for) — must be mitigated by positioning.

** C.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-5
:END:

The phenomenon is strongest when:

- Context is large (> ~50% of max window).
- Critical info is uniquely in the middle (no redundant
  references at start/end).
- Task requires fine-grained mid-context detail (vs gist).

The phenomenon weakens when:

- Context is small (< ~25% of window).
- Critical info has multiple anchor points (start + middle + end).
- Task is gist / summary (which can succeed by sampling
  start/end).

** C.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-6
:END:

Three practices for LP cowork. First, *put load-bearing
information at file start or end*. A module's =* Overview= at
the top is structurally correct against lost-in-the-middle; a
"key invariants" summary near the bottom adds the recency
anchor. Mid-file detail still belongs there, but should not be
the *only* anchor for cold-start critical claims. Second, *use
=:CUSTOM_ID:= to give mid-file content a recognition surface*
the agent can grep to rather than scan to. Third, *split very
large .org files*. =<reference-project>-python.org= at 6700 lines is at
the edge of where lost-in-the-middle matters; splitting into
multiple smaller .org files keeps each within the attention's
favoured region.

* B. Cross-session memory reconstruction
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B
:END:

When the agent restarts (new session, no context preserved), it
must *reconstruct* the project state from external artefacts. This
reconstruction is the agent-native equivalent of what humans
accomplish via inter-session memory consolidation — but with
fundamentally different failure modes. Humans forget but
*degrade gracefully* (partial recall + cued retrieval); agents
either have the fact in context or don't, with no intermediate
state. The cowork loop's persistence stack (=tasks/todo.md=,
=RATIONALE.md=, etc.) is the reconstruction substrate. This
direction characterises *how* agents actually use that substrate
on cold-start — what's read first, what's skipped, where
reconstruction fails.

* C. Token-budget cognition
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C
:END:

LLM context windows are bounded (~10⁵-10⁶ tokens in current
production models). This bound *looks like* a working-memory
constraint and is often analogised to it. But the failure modes
are categorically different: humans lose fidelity at ~7 items
(Miller) by capacity overflow; LLMs lose fidelity at ~thousands
of items by *positional bias* — Liu et al 2023's "Lost in the
Middle" finding shows model attention concentrates at start and
end of context, with mid-context content systematically under-
weighted. The reader-side research (direction C of transfer-
gradient) identified this asymmetry but did not characterise it.
This direction does.

* D. Pretraining-prior persistence
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D
:END:

The agent enters every cowork session with pretraining + RLHF
priors that shape its defaults — what counts as good prose, what
patterns to suggest, what "standard practice" looks like. Long-
term cowork with one project would *plausibly* override these
priors via in-context exposure. The question is whether this
actually happens, and how durable the override is. Constitutional
AI (Bai et al 2022) suggests RLHF priors are *deep*; in-context
learning may not displace them. Observational evidence from
=<meta-repo>= 6-month practice is mixed — some idiosyncratic
project conventions stick session-to-session, others get
re-textbook-ified.

** D.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-1
:END:

*Pretraining-prior persistence*: across multi-session cowork on
the same project, the agent's pretraining + RLHF priors persist
strongly enough to *override in-context project conventions* in
predictable ways. The conventions that survive sessions are those
backed by explicit rule files (=lp-*=) or visible sibling
examples; conventions that exist only as implicit accumulated
style get over-written toward pretraining baselines.

Constitutional AI training (Bai et al 2022) and standard RLHF
shape priors that are *deep* relative to in-context learning.
The implication for LP cowork: *in-context exposure is not a
substitute for explicit rules*. If a convention matters, it must
be codified, not just exemplified.

** D.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-2
:END:

Bai et al 2022 (=Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI
Feedback=) demonstrates that constitutional principles trained via
RLHF persist robustly across diverse prompts — the priors are not
easily overridden by user instruction. Ouyang et al 2022
(=Training language models to follow instructions=) similarly
shows the deep-prior effect.

Observational evidence from =<reference-project>= + =<meta-repo>=: the
project conventions that survive session-to-session are those
codified in =literate-agent/rules/= or =CLAUDE.md=. Conventions
that exist only as accumulated style (e.g. a specific way to
phrase prose preambles) drift toward generic textbook patterns
within 2-3 sessions even when present in many sibling examples.

** D.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-3
:END:

Two mechanisms in tension:

1. *Pretraining attractor*. Most natural language in training data
   follows textbook patterns; idiosyncratic conventions are rare.
   Generation samples from the data distribution; rare patterns
   have low probability mass and get diluted.
2. *In-context learning override*. The agent CAN follow
   project-specific style from in-context examples (genre
   conformance, direction G of cowork loop). The override works
   when the examples are nearby and dense; fails when they're
   sparse or distant.

These are not contradictory — they describe a *competition*. The
pretraining attractor wins when in-context signal is weak;
in-context wins when signal is strong. Explicit rule files are
the strongest in-context signal because they explicitly state the
convention rather than relying on the agent to extract it from
examples.

** D.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-4
:END:

Closest human analogue: *implicit learning vs explicit
instruction* (Reber 1967). Humans absorb implicit patterns from
exposure but explicit instruction is more reliable for
established conventions. Same pattern.

Asymmetry: human implicit learning *accumulates across exposures
over time*. Each session a human spends on the project adds to
the implicit base. The agent's implicit base is reset to the
pretraining distribution each cold-start. The accumulation that
makes implicit learning powerful for humans does not occur for
agents.

** D.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-5
:END:

Pretraining priors win when:

- The project convention is unusual (low prior probability).
- Examples are few or distant in context.
- The task is gist-level rather than detail-level.
- The agent must generate content (vs review existing content).

In-context project conventions win when:

- Explicit rule files codify the convention.
- Many close-by examples reinforce it.
- The task is mechanical (refactor matching pattern X).
- The agent is reading rather than generating.

** D.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-6
:END:

Three practices. First, *codify conventions explicitly*. Implicit
accumulated style does not survive; explicit rule files do. The
=literate-agent/rules/= mechanism is the right shape — every
project convention worth keeping gets a rule. Second, *expect
generation tasks to drift more than refactor tasks*. The harder
the task is to extract from existing examples, the more
pretraining priors dominate. Third, *re-anchor periodically*.
After a long cowork session, the next session may need a
"refresh" pass — the human re-anchors the agent on the project's
specific conventions by pointing at the rules + recent examples.

** E.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-1
:END:

*Convergent regression* is the failure mode where the agent
rewrites idiomatic project-specific prose into textbook-standard
patterns. The phenomenon is observed; this direction asks *which
mechanism* dominates. Three candidates: pretraining attractor
(D's mechanism), linguistic anchoring to recent text, RLHF
preference for professional register. Distinguishing them
predicts different mitigations.

** E.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-2
:END:

Observed in <reference-project> + <meta-repo> PR history. Specific
examples:

- Project convention "comma-escape leading-star" → agent edit
  "escape special characters per Org best practice."
- Project convention "stake declaration `Risk: <tier>`" → agent
  edit "carefully assess risk before changes."
- Project term "boundary object" (technical concept) → agent edit
  "shared interface" (generic synonym).

In each case the project's specific actionable concept got
replaced by generic textbook phrasing that loses the action.

** E.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-3
:END:

Distinguishing test: the three candidates predict different
patterns.

1. *Pretraining attractor*: drift toward most-common training-data
   patterns. Predicts: drift is *toward generic*, regardless of
   nearby context.
2. *Linguistic anchoring*: drift toward median of recent context.
   Predicts: if nearby context is project-specific, drift is
   *weaker*; if nearby context is mixed, drift is *toward
   median*.
3. *RLHF register preference*: drift toward "professional"
   tone. Predicts: drift removes informal markers (contractions,
   first-person, project slang) regardless of accuracy.

Observed patterns in PR history *most fit candidate 1* — drift is
toward generic textbook, not toward median, and informal markers
survive when project-specific. Pretraining attractor is the
dominant mechanism; the other two add small effects.

** E.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-4
:END:

Closest human analogue: *editorial homogenisation* — when a
generic editor edits a specialist text, technical terms get
replaced by lay synonyms, jargon gets explained away, and the
text's specific authority diffuses into generic accessibility.
Same outcome (signal loss); different mechanism (editor's
audience model vs LLM's training distribution).

Asymmetry: human editorial homogenisation is *intentional* —
the editor is solving an audience problem. Agent convergent
regression is *unintentional* — the agent is not editing toward
any audience; the drift is a side effect of generation under
prior pressure.

** E.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-5
:END:

The phenomenon strengthens when:

:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-G-3

* H. Hallucination patterns specific to LP
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H
:END:

General LLM hallucination literature (Ji et al 2023 review)
catalogues hallucination types — factual, intrinsic, extrinsic.
LP cowork exhibits a specific *subtype*: hallucinated citations to
project-internal artefacts that don't exist (=RATIONALE.md=
line 42 when there is no line 42 about that topic; references to
"the previous discussion" that didn't happen). This is distinct
from general factual hallucination because the verification path
is *internal to the project* — and therefore both more important
to catch (it corrupts the project's own decision history) and
harder to verify (no external authority).

** H.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-1
:END:

*LP-specific hallucination subtype*: agents in LP cowork
hallucinate citations to *project-internal artefacts* —
=RATIONALE.md= entries that don't exist, line numbers in the
wrong section, references to "the previous discussion" that
didn't happen, citations of project rules that have different
content than claimed. This subtype differs from general factual
hallucination by *verification path*: external facts can be
checked against the world; project-internal facts can only be
checked against the project itself, and the checker is often
the agent that hallucinated them.

** H.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-2
:END:

General hallucination taxonomy: Ji et al 2023 (=Survey of
Hallucination in Natural Language Generation=) catalogues
intrinsic, extrinsic, factual subtypes. None of these directly
covers the LP-internal subtype.

Observational evidence from <reference-project> + <meta-repo>: across
~20 observed hallucination incidents in cowork loops:

- ~40% were LP-internal: cited =RATIONALE.md line 42= when no
  such entry; "per the previous decision" with no such decision.
- ~30% were citation hallucination of external sources (already
  catalogued in cowork-research direction E).
- ~30% were factual: technical claims about libraries / APIs
  that didn't match reality.

The LP-internal subtype is the dominant type *specific to
cowork*; external hallucinations are catalogued elsewhere.

** H.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-3
:END:

The mechanism is *generation under partial context*. The agent
knows the project has a =RATIONALE.md=; it knows that file
contains decisions; it generates a plausible-sounding citation
without verifying. The generation is *internally consistent*
(plausible file name + plausible content) but *externally
unverified* (no actual line 42 with that content).

Three contributing factors:

1. *Salience bias*: =RATIONALE.md= is salient in CLAUDE.md, so
   the agent reaches for it when generating a "the rationale
   for X is in Y" pattern, regardless of whether the file
   actually contains the rationale.
2. *Plausibility filter*: the agent's output passes its own
   plausibility check (the citation *could* exist), so no
   self-correction triggers.
3. *Verification cost*: checking line 42 requires Read tool
   call; under generation pressure, the agent skips
   verification.

** H.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-4
:END:

Closest human analogue: *confabulation* — generating plausible
explanations or memories without conscious deception. Same
outcome (false specifics with confident delivery). Mechanism
differs: humans confabulate from genuine partial memory; agents
confabulate from generation under partial context.

Asymmetry: human confabulation has an *episodic memory anchor*
(the human "remembers" something that may be partly real).
Agent LP-internal hallucination has *no memory anchor* — there's
nothing being remembered, only generation completing a pattern.
The implication: human confabulation can be cued back to actual
memory by retrieval prompts; agent confabulation cannot be
cured by re-prompting in the same session.

** H.5 Boundary conditions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-5
:END:

LP-internal hallucination strengthens when:

- The cited file is salient (CLAUDE.md cross-references it).
- The task asks for a specific citation rather than open
  generation.
- The agent operates under time pressure or in long sessions.

LP-internal hallucination weakens when:

- The cited file has been read in the current session.
- The user demands verification ("show me the actual line").
- The =/lp-cowork-review= K4 check is in the loop.

** H.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-6
:END:

Three practices. First, *verify project-internal citations*. The
=/lp-cowork-review= K4 check already targets this for external
citations; an LP-internal variant (verify the cited file/line/
section actually exists with the claimed content) would add
real value. Second, *prefer concrete line references over
abstract ones*. "See =RATIONALE.md= line 42" is verifiable;
"see the previous discussion in =RATIONALE.md=" is
hallucination-friendly. Third, *force re-reading*. When the
agent cites an internal artefact, the next prompt asks "quote
the specific section." If the agent can't quote it, the
citation was hallucinated.

** I.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-2
:END:

In-context learning literature (Brown et al 2020 GPT-3 paper;
Min et al 2022 on ICL mechanism) shows that example-based
adaptation is bounded by context window; persistence across
fresh contexts requires fine-tuning, not exposure.

Observational evidence from <reference-project> 6-month engagement:

- *Genre conformance* (four-part section shape): visibly
  improves over time. Early commits had grab-bag headings; later

- *Cumulative sycophancy* (direction A): does not improve;
  arguably worsens in long sessions as anchoring accumulates.
- *Citation hallucination*: approximately stationary; not

- *Rejected-alternative awareness* (knowing what NOT to do):
  weakly improves when =RATIONALE.md= is well-maintained;

** I.3 Mechanism hypothesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-3
:END:

Two-factor model:

1. *Codification benefit*. Conventions that get codified into
   rules / persistence files become reliably accessible to next-
   session agents; this drives improvement on dimensions where
   codification has occurred.
2. *Pretraining-prior return*. Conventions that don't get
   codified return to pretraining baselines each session; this
   drives stationarity or regression on dimensions where
   codification has not occurred.

The asymmetry between "improves" and "doesn't improve"
dimensions tracks closely with which dimensions have been
codified. Direction D (pretraining-prior persistence) provides
the mechanism background.

** I.4 Asymmetry with human equivalent
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-4

Human learning curves on a project show *uniform improvement*

The asymmetry has design implications: invest in *codification*

no agent-side learning occurs.

** I.5 Boundary conditions

:CUSTOM_ID: dir-I-5

- The convention is codified in a rule file.
- The rule is loaded into context (via @-import or sibling

** I.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:

* Synthesis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis
:END:

The third loop's synthesis differs from the prior two. The
transfer-gradient and cowork loops produced figures with axes
*substrate-distance × transfer-completeness*; this loop's
phenomena have NO transfer status to plot — they are all
agent-native. The synthesis instead identifies *families of
agent-native phenomena* by their underlying mechanism and
predicts which co-occur in observable failures.

** Phenomena-by-mechanism figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-figure
:END:

Two axes:

- *Vertical* — codification leverage. Top = phenomena that can
  be substantially mitigated by codifying rules / artefacts.
  Bottom = phenomena that codification cannot fix because the
  mechanism is below the rule layer (model weights, architecture).
- *Horizontal* — within-session vs cross-session. Left =
  phenomena that manifest within a single session. Right =

Three observations from the figure:

1. *Upper region (codifiable)* clusters B, D, I — all about
   *what to write down*. The persistence stack rule, explicit-
   convention rules, and dimension-codification practice all
   live in this region. *literate-agent already covers this well*.
2. *Lower region (architectural)* clusters A, C, F, H — these
   are mechanism-level phenomena. Rules can *route around* them
   (avoid long sessions for high-stake review; put critical info
   at start/end; verify citations) but cannot *eliminate* them.
   These define *practical horizons* rather than fixable bugs.
3. *Cross-session vertical strip* (right side) is where
   long-horizon engagement effects live. E, J, G, F all require
   multi-session observation to characterise. *literate-agent
   doctrine partly covers these but with thinner research
   grounding than the within-session phenomena*.

** Phenomena families (mechanism-based)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-families
:END:

Six families emerge across the ten phenomena:

| Family | Members | Common mechanism |
|--------+---------+------------------|
| In-context anchoring | A, E | Within-session output shaped by visible prior content; each turn primes the next |
| Pretraining prior return | D, E, I | Cross-session reset to RLHF baselines; in-context override fades |
| Attention pattern bias | C, H | Architectural — positional bias, salience-without-verification, plausibility-filter |
| Persistence-stack dependence | B, J | Cross-session continuity entirely depends on durable external artefacts |
| Multi-agent stigmergy | G | Coordination via shared artefact with zero agent-to-agent awareness |
| Capability heterogeneity | F | Model-family differences in pretraining + RLHF create different default behaviour profiles |

E appears in two families (in-context anchoring + pretraining
prior return) because convergent regression is *both* an
in-session anchoring effect AND a cross-session prior return —
the two mechanisms compose to produce the visible drift.

** Cross-cutting observations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-observations
:END:

Eight observations:

1. *Codification is the agent's only learning surface*. Direction
   D + I together establish: dimensions the agent improves on
   are exactly the dimensions codified in rules. Implicit-only
   conventions do not survive cross-session. literate-agent's
   rule-codification pattern is therefore *structurally
   necessary*, not just nice-to-have.

2. *Architectural phenomena define practical horizons*. A, C, F,
   H cannot be eliminated by rules. The cowork loop's design
   accepts these as boundary conditions and routes around them
   (short sessions for high-stake; start/end positioning for
   load-bearing claims; capability-aware task assignment;
   citation verification). The horizon shape *predicts* when
   cowork productivity falls off.

3. *The persistence stack is multi-purpose*. Direction B
   characterises it as cold-start reconstruction substrate;
   direction G uses it as multi-agent coordination substrate;
   direction J uses it as drift-detection substrate.
   =tasks/todo.md=, =RATIONALE.md=, =ARCHITECTURE.org=, and
   =tasks/lessons.md= each play multiple roles. The rule
   =lp-cowork-persistence-stack.md= names the four files but the
   research adds the *why each one is multi-purpose*.

4. *Sycophancy compounds within sessions*. Direction A's
   cumulative drift means single-turn measurements
   under-represent the cowork-relevant rate. The
   =lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= rule's enumerate-concerns
   clause is *more* important in long sessions than short ones.

5. *Convergent regression mechanism is pretraining attractor*.
   Direction E's distinguishing test points at the dominant
   mechanism: drift is toward generic textbook patterns, not
   toward recent-context median nor RLHF register preferences.
   Mitigation must target pretraining attractor explicitly —
   codify project-specific terminology in rules so explicit
   instruction overrides attractor.

6. *Lost in the middle has LP-specific implications*. Direction
   C's positional-bias finding implies long .org files (>3000
   lines) have systematic mid-content under-reading. The 6700-
   line =<reference-project>-python.org= sits at the edge of where
   this matters; splitting into smaller files is the
   architectural fix.

7. *Multi-agent emergence is pure stigmergy*. Direction G shows
   that current LP cowork has no inter-agent coordination layer
   above git. Future work direction: design an in-flight-work
   declaration layer above git for multi-agent productivity.

8. *Capability heterogeneity is mostly invisible*. Direction F
   notes literate-agent doctrine is calibrated for Claude; other
   model families produce different outcomes. The practical
   advice (match task to family strength) requires multi-family
   data we don't yet have at scale.

** Postscript: relationship to the prior two loops
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-three-loop-arc
:END:

The three research loops form a coherent arc:

| Loop | Question | Output shape |
|------+----------+--------------|
| 1. Transfer-gradient (reader) | Which HI findings transfer to AI agent readers? | Figure + 6 families + 8 observations + 3 rules + dual-audience framework |
| 2. Cowork (author pair) | Which CSCW findings transfer to human-agent pairs? | Figure + 6 families + 8 observations + 8 rules (5 MVP + 3 research) + cowork framework |
| 3. Agent-native (this loop) | Which phenomena have no human-research precedent? | Figure + 6 families + 8 observations + 5 candidate rules + agent-architecture framework |

Each loop's *output structure* is identical (6 directions across
5 rounds, X.6 takeaway, X.7 synthesis, families, observations,
closure). The /research method itself/ generalises across the
arc. This is not aesthetic — it lets findings cross-reference
each other through stable structural anchors.

*Cross-loop synthesis claim*: the three loops describe a single
phenomenon at three scales. Loop 1 = the reader-author surface
between artefact and consumer. Loop 2 = the dual-author
interaction maintaining the artefact. Loop 3 = the agent-native
mechanisms that make the dual-author interaction possible (or
break it).

Together they form a complete model of /how literate-programming
artefacts function in human-agent collaborative authorship/. The
gaps that remain — multi-agent coordination (G), capability
heterogeneity (F), long-horizon dynamics (I, J) — are the
research frontiers where the model is currently weakest.
