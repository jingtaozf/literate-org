* Preface
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: preface

This research extends the transfer-gradient loop
(see =literate-agent/docs/transfer-gradient.org=) from the *reader*
side to the *author* side. The earlier loop asked /which human-
interface findings transfer to AI agent readers?/ and produced
the dual-audience design framework now codified in
=literate-agent/rules/lp-*= and =literate-agent/hints/dual-audience-
checklist.org=. The present loop asks the symmetric question for
*authors*: /which CSCW + co-authoring findings transfer from human
+ human pairs to human + AI agent pairs?/

Five MVP rules shipped before this loop began (see
=literate-agent/rules/lp-cowork-*=). Those rules were derived from
6+ months of observed practice across =<reference-project>= + =<meta-repo>=
+ =cmux=. This loop's purpose is to *validate and deepen* the MVP,
not to bootstrap from scratch. Each direction will either ground a
rule in pre-existing research, or surface a new pattern the MVP
missed.

** Scope
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: scope
:END:

Ten directions across five rounds of progressive depth. Eight are
classical CSCW / co-authoring; two (C, E) are LLM-specific because
*sycophancy* and *trust calibration* are cowork failure modes with
no human-human precedent strong enough to anchor on.

Per direction, six questions (same shape as the reader-side loop):

1. /Specific claim/ of the finding (named principle + canonical wording).
2. /Empirical basis/ (study, N, method, year).
3. Does the human + AI agent author pair show isomorphic behaviour?
   Evidence from observed practice, agent traces, or prior literature?
4. If yes — /shared primitive/?
5. If no — /transfer break/? Substrate mismatch, asymmetric capability,
   tacit-knowledge gap, stake mismatch?
6. /Operational takeaway/ for the LP cowork author.

* A. Articulation work
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A
:END:

Articulation work is the *invisible coordination labour* that
keeps a multi-actor task on track — communicating who's doing
what, surfacing dependencies, repairing breakdowns. Suchman 1987
named it; Schmidt & Bannon 1992 made it the founding concept of
CSCW. In a LP cowork loop, articulation work is what the human and
agent do when they're not directly editing the artefact: declaring
intent, checking handoffs, recovering from drift. The transfer-
gradient question is whether the agent participates symmetrically
in this labour or whether the human carries it disproportionately.

** A.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-1
:END:

Schmidt & Bannon 1992 (=CSCW 1(1)=, founding issue of the journal)
formulated the claim: /any cooperative task generates a second-
order task — articulating who does what, when, in what sequence,
and how outputs assemble. This articulation work is itself
substantive labour, not friction to be eliminated./ Strauss 1985
(=Work and the Division of Labor=) introduced the term in
sociology of work; Suchman 1987 (=Plans and Situated Actions=)
brought it into HCI by showing that automated systems systematically
under-supply articulation work and humans compensate invisibly.

The sharp form for cowork: *the visible primary task (writing the
LP doc) is accompanied by an invisible secondary task (deciding
who writes which section, what order, how edits compose) — and
the secondary task does not vanish when one partner is an AI agent;
it shifts onto whichever partner notices it first.*

** A.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-2
:END:

Suchman 1987 was ethnographic: observations of office workers
using a Xerox copier whose "intelligent" help system fails to
participate in articulation work. Strauss 1985 was a synthesis
across sociological case studies (hospitals, scientific labs).
Schmidt & Bannon 1992 was a programmatic statement at CSCW's
founding, citing prior ethnographies rather than presenting fresh
empirical data. The category is well-anchored in observational
sociology with N ranging from single-case ethnography to
multi-site comparative studies.

For LP cowork specifically, the empirical layer is the host
project's own 6+ months of practice: =<reference-project>= +
=<meta-repo>= + =cmux= commit histories, the =tasks/lessons.md=
files, and =RATIONALE.md= entries that *visibly record* the
articulation labour the agent and human shared across PR cycles.
A grep for "we tried X" / "decided Y because" / "deferred Z" hits
hundreds of times across the three repos — that is the
articulation-work substrate made visible by writing it down.

** A.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-3
:END:

Does the human-agent pair perform articulation work, and does the
agent participate symmetrically? Three observations from
=<reference-project>= practice:

1. *The agent will perform articulation work when asked* — when the
   human's prompt explicitly requests planning, sequencing, or
   handoff articulation, the agent produces it with high quality.
2. *The agent will NOT perform articulation work proactively* — left
   to its own pacing, the agent executes the requested task and
   returns without surfacing the articulation context. The human
   then carries it invisibly (deciding what's next, what's deferred,
   what's been tried).
3. *Articulation work resurfaces as failure*. When the agent fails
   to read the persistence stack (`tasks/todo.md`, `RATIONALE.md`),
   the next session reproduces a discussion already had. The
   articulation labour was done before but lost — re-doing it
   feels like progress but is actually wasted motion.

The transfer is *asymmetric*: the agent participates as a tool used
to perform articulation, but does not initiate it. Hence the MVP
rules (=lp-cowork-handoff-template.md=, =-persistence-stack.md=)
exist — they shift articulation-work initiation from "implicit
human responsibility" to "explicit shared protocol."

** A.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-4
:END:

The shared primitive is: *any multi-actor task with non-trivial
state generates articulation work proportional to the state size,
and the work must be explicitly assigned to one actor or it
silently falls on whoever cares most.* The primitive does not care
which substrate the actor runs on — Suchman's office workers,
Strauss's hospital nurses, and the human + agent author pair all
face the same constraint.

The transfer holds because articulation work is *behavioural*: it
exists whenever the task's components must be coordinated, and the
amount of work is set by the task structure, not by the actors'
internal cognition. As long as both actors can read and write to
a shared surface (here, the LP file + persistence stack), the
primitive applies.

** A.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-5
:END:

The transfer breaks at the *initiation* boundary. Human-human
pairs spontaneously articulate ("I'll take the auth section if you
take the cmux backend"); human-agent pairs do not, because the
agent's defaults bias toward fast execution rather than
articulation surfacing. This is the same dual-process pattern the
reader-side research catalogued (direction E) — agents default
to fast mode unless the slow-mode trigger is explicit.

The break is mitigated, not eliminated, by MVP rules. With
=stake-declaration= + =propose-before-edit= + =handoff-template=,
the human still initiates the articulation but the agent's
response carries the required structure. Without them, the agent
returns a finished edit and the articulation labour falls on the
human reviewer 100%.

** A.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-A-6
:END:

Three practices follow from articulation work theory for the LP
cowork author. First, *make articulation labour visible and
splittable*. The persistence stack (=tasks/todo.md= +
=tasks/lessons.md= + =ARCHITECTURE.org= + =RATIONALE.md=) is a
materialised articulation surface — both populations write to it,
both populations read it. Second, *initiate articulation
explicitly*. The agent will produce planning prose on request;
asking is the lever that triggers the labour. The MVP =propose-
before-edit= rule mechanises this trigger. Third, *do not let
articulation re-cost across sessions*. The handoff-template rule
captures the articulation outcomes in writeable form so the next
session starts where the last one ended, not re-litigating.

* B. Common ground
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B
:END:

Common ground is the *shared belief and mutual assumption* that
two interlocutors maintain as they communicate. Clark 1996 (=Using
Language=) made it the keystone of conversational coordination:
every utterance is interpreted against what speaker and hearer
both presume the other knows. In LP cowork, common ground is what
the human and agent share about the project's state, conventions,
and rejected alternatives — and *how that shared state is
maintained when the agent restarts with no memory*. The transfer
question is whether external artefacts (the LP files themselves)
can stand in for the inter-session common ground a human-human
pair builds incrementally.

** B.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-1
:END:

Clark 1996 (=Using Language=) names the claim: /every utterance
is interpreted against /common ground/ — the shared belief speaker
and hearer mutually presume the other knows/. Clark & Brennan
1991 added the /grounding constraints/ formulation: each
communication medium has different costs for the seven grounding
actions (formulate / produce / receive / understand / acknowledge
/ display / repair), and partners must use a medium-appropriate
strategy to keep common ground.

The sharp form for LP cowork: *the LP file IS the persisted
common-ground artefact. Edits to it are grounding actions.
Cross-session common ground depends on whether the file's
structure makes the next reader's understanding cheap enough to
re-establish.*

** B.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-2
:END:

Clark 1996 synthesises ~20 years of psycholinguistic experiments
on dialogue (including the classic Schober & Clark 1989 referring-
expression studies, N ≈ 30 dyads). Clark & Brennan 1991 is a
theory paper grounded in the same experimental tradition. The
empirical claims about cost asymmetry across media (telephone,
face-to-face, email, text chat) have been replicated extensively;
the founding cost-of-grounding framework holds across studies.

For LP cowork, the parallel evidence is observational: PR review
cycles in =<reference-project>= show grounding failure when the agent
arrives at a file fresh and the file's =* Overview= prose does
not establish enough common ground — the agent either over-reads
code to recover context (cost: tokens) or makes an inconsistent
edit (cost: rework). Modules with rich prose preambles produce
visibly fewer such failures.

** B.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-3
:END:

The agent exhibits Clark's *least collaborative effort principle*
asymmetrically. In a single session, the agent freely makes
references like "as we discussed above" / "per the previous
edit" — building local common ground efficiently. Across sessions,
this collapses because the agent has no inter-session memory; the
common ground that humans accumulate through repeated reading
must be *re-established by the file's persistent structure* on
each cold start.

The asymmetry maps cleanly onto the dual-audience model: the
human reader maintains common ground via mental-model accretion
(see transfer-gradient direction E); the agent reader maintains
it via *recognition surfaces in the artefact* (CUSTOM_ID anchors,
concept headings, overview prose). Both populations end up at the
same place — common ground established — but by different
substrates.

** B.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-4
:END:

The shared primitive is: *understanding any utterance/edit
requires common ground; the cost of grounding is proportional to
the medium's cost structure; partners adopt strategies that
minimise expected cost given the medium they're in.* The primitive
is medium-relative but not substrate-relative — it applies to
any pair of communicators whose channel has finite grounding cost.

For LP cowork, the medium is the LP file plus the conversational
turn around it. The structural anchors (CUSTOM_ID, concept
heading, overview prose) reduce grounding cost; the absence of
those anchors increases it. The =lp-cowork-handoff-template.md=
rule's three templates ARE specifications of low-cost grounding
strategies for the three transition points.

** B.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-5
:END:

The transfer breaks at /repair/. Clark's framework distinguishes
seven grounding actions; the seventh is *repair* — fixing a
misunderstanding when it surfaces. Human-human repair is mutual
("wait, did you mean X?"), incremental, and cheap. Human-agent
repair is asymmetric: the human notices most misunderstandings
because the agent's confidence is poorly calibrated to its
correctness. The agent rarely says "wait, did you mean X?" in
the human's sense — instead it confidently produces output that
contains the misunderstanding.

This is the same break the =lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= MVP
rule mitigates, viewed from the grounding-theory angle: the rule
*forces the agent to surface candidate misunderstandings*
explicitly, even when its confidence does not trigger spontaneous
repair.

** B.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-B-6
:END:

Three practices follow from common-ground theory for the LP
cowork author. First, *invest the grounding cost upfront in the
overview prose*. Every module's =* Overview= section is the
file's primary grounding artefact; rich preambles trade
authoring time for every-future-session grounding savings.
Second, *use medium-appropriate strategies for the cross-session
medium*. The LP file is asynchronous, persistent, and structurally
rich — strategies that exploit structure (CUSTOM_ID, named
blocks, cross-references) beat strategies that exploit recency
("as I just said"). Third, *force repair surfacing*. The
anti-sycophancy rule's "enumerate ≥ 1 specific concern" clause
turns the agent's silent confidence into an explicit grounding
check.

** C.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-1
:END:

Lee & See 2004 (=Human Factors 46(1)=) named the claim: /reliance
on automation should track the automation's actual reliability.
Three failure modes exist — /under-trust/ (rejects working
automation), /over-trust/ (relies on failing automation), and
/uncalibrated trust/ (reliance has no relationship to reliability).
Calibration is a measurable property of the human-automation pair,
distinct from the human's general disposition or the automation's
intrinsic capability./

Hoff & Bashir 2015 (=Human Factors 57(3)=) reviewed 16 years of
literature, organising trust antecedents into /dispositional/ +
/situational/ + /learned/ layers — useful because LP cowork trust
is dominantly /learned/ (calibrated session by session through
observed agent outputs).

** C.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-2
:END:

Lee & See 2004 was a theory synthesis citing ~150 prior empirical
studies of human-automation interaction (mostly cockpit and
process-control settings). The three-mode trust-failure taxonomy
was operationalised in subsequent measurement work (e.g. Lewandowsky
et al 2000 on under-trust; Parasuraman & Riley 1997 on over-trust).
Hoff & Bashir 2015 reviewed 101 studies; the dispositional /
situational / learned decomposition is replicated across them.

For LP cowork the empirical parallel is observational: PR cycles
show all three failure modes. Under-trust: human re-verifies
trivially-correct agent edits, wasting cycles. Over-trust: human
accepts agent citations without checking, codifying fake
references. Uncalibrated: human's "do I trust this output" check
fires randomly across the diff regardless of actual risk.

** C.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-3
:END:

Calibration is fundamentally a *property of the human's reliance
behaviour*, not the agent's behaviour. The agent does not have
its own calibration to track — the question is whether the
*human's* trust signals correctly distinguish reliable vs
unreliable agent outputs.

Two observations from LP cowork practice:

1. *Agent outputs are not uniformly reliable across task types*.
   Mechanical refactor: high reliability. Citation lookup:
   moderate reliability (verification recommended). Taste
   judgment about prose voice: low reliability (always defer to
   human). The human's calibration must distinguish these.
2. *The agent's surface confidence does not track reliability*.
   Sycophantic agreement reads confident; an honest "I don't have
   a way to verify this" reads uncertain — but the latter is
   often more reliable. Calibrated trust requires reading past
   the confidence surface to the actual provenance of the claim.

The =lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= rule's "either enumerate
concerns OR re-derive central claim" forces the agent to *display
its provenance*, giving the human a calibration signal.

** C.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-4
:END:

The shared primitive is: *appropriate reliance requires the human
to have a model of where the automation succeeds and fails, and
the automation must surface evidence the human can use to build
that model.* The primitive is general across human-automation
pairs (cockpit, process control, LP cowork) because the constraint
is *information flow about reliability*, not the cognitive
mechanism of trust.

** C.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-5
:END:

The transfer is partial. Lee & See's cockpit-automation literature
assumes a /finite, enumerable set of automation modes/ each with
roughly stable reliability — autopilot is "X% accurate on Y task
class." LLM agents have *no finite enumerable mode set*; the same
prompt can produce reliable output one turn and unreliable the
next as context shifts. Calibration in the strict cockpit sense
cannot exist because the underlying random variable is not
stationary.

The mitigation literature on this point is in the LLM literature
itself rather than the classical automation-trust literature —
see direction E (sycophancy measurement) for the agent-side
view.

** C.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C-6
:END:

Three practices for the LP cowork author. First, *force the agent
to display provenance*. Anti-sycophancy + propose-before-edit
both expose the agent's reasoning surface so the human's trust
signal has evidence to work on. Second, *task-type calibration
beats global calibration*. Track reliability separately for
mechanical refactor (default trust), citation lookup (verify),
taste judgment (defer). Third, *anti-cockpit assumption*. The
agent's reliability is not stationary; recalibrate per session
rather than per relationship.

* C. Trust calibration
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-C
:END:

Trust calibration is the *match between a human operator's reliance
on an automated system and the system's actual reliability*. Lee &
See 2004 (=Human Factors=) named the three failure modes —
under-trust (rejects working automation), over-trust (relies on
failing automation), and uncalibrated trust (no relationship
between reliance and reliability). For LP cowork, the question is
how the human's reliance on the agent's prose / refactor / citation
should track the agent's actual reliability on each. The
=lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= rule is the first-order mitigation;
this direction tests whether calibration research suggests deeper
moves.

* D. Code review dynamics
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D
:END:

Code review is the most empirically studied form of cowork in
software engineering. Bacchelli & Bird 2013 (=ICSE=) named the
gap between /expectations/ and /outcomes/: reviewers and authors
agree review's purpose is defect-finding, but the dominant
realised outcome is *knowledge transfer + code style enforcement*.
Czerwonka et al 2015 (=Microsoft Research=) added the large-scale
empirical layer. For LP cowork, the agent participating in PR
review faces the same gap — what reviews are *expected* to do
versus what they *actually* do shape which findings transfer.

** D.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-1
:END:

Bacchelli & Bird 2013 (=ICSE '13=) framed the claim: /developers
state code review's purpose is defect-finding, but observed
outcomes are dominated by knowledge transfer + code style + design
improvement. The gap between stated and realised function is
substantial./ Czerwonka et al 2015 added the Microsoft-scale
empirical layer: median reviews touch ~2 files, find ~1 issue,
and serve mostly as knowledge propagation through the team.

Sharp form for LP cowork: *the human-agent review pair inherits
the same gap. The human asks the agent to find defects; the
agent's main realised contribution is knowledge transfer (catching
inconsistency with prior decisions, surfacing rejected
alternatives) and style enforcement (deletion-test prose, structural
signifier density). Defect-finding is a tertiary outcome.*

** D.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-2
:END:

Bacchelli & Bird 2013 surveyed 165 developers and conducted 17
interviews at Microsoft. Czerwonka et al 2015 analysed 24,000+
review threads. The two studies converge on the
expectation-outcome gap.

For LP cowork, the parallel evidence: =<reference-project>= PR history
shows agent review comments are dominated by (a) structural-rule
violations (CLAUDE.md @-import drift, missing :CUSTOM_ID:, etc.) —
which are *style* in Bacchelli's taxonomy — and (b) flagging
sections that contradict earlier decisions documented in
=RATIONALE.md= — which is *knowledge transfer*. Defect-finding
in the "this code is buggy" sense is rare; defect-finding in the
"this prose claim contradicts your earlier rule" sense is common.

** D.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-3
:END:

The agent reviewer fills the *knowledge transfer + style* roles
better than the *defect-finding* role for two structural reasons:

1. *Knowledge transfer*: the agent has effectively read every file
   in the project in a session (or can re-read cheaply). Spotting
   "this contradicts =RATIONALE.md= line 42" is the agent's
   strong suit because the cost of recall is uniform per token
   regardless of when the relevant fact was written.
2. *Style*: structural rules are mechanically checkable.
   =/lp-research-audit= and =/lp-cowork-review= commands
   mechanise much of this. The agent applies rules consistently;
   reviewers in Bacchelli's study reported style enforcement was
   one of their dominant realised behaviours too.
3. *Defect-finding*: the agent's "this code is buggy" signal is
   poorly calibrated because the agent cannot run the code and
   observe behaviour; semantic intuition lacks the runtime
   anchor.

The transfer is partial: gap-shape (expectation > outcomes) holds,
and outcome-shape (knowledge transfer + style > defect-finding)
holds, but the agent's relative strengths within outcome-shape
differ from the human reviewer's. Specifically, agents have a
strong recall-driven knowledge-transfer profile; humans have a
strong intuition-driven defect-finding profile.

** D.4 Shared primitive (if transfer holds)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-4
:END:

The shared primitive is: *code review is a multi-purpose tool
whose realised outcomes depend on the reviewer's capability
profile, with knowledge transfer + style typically dominant
because they are cheaper to produce than defect-finding.* The
primitive is reviewer-substrate-independent — the cost-shape
that produces the expectation-outcome gap holds for human and
agent reviewers alike.

** D.5 Where transfer breaks (if it does)
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-5
:END:

The break is at /implicit knowledge of project context/. Bacchelli
documented that effective review depends on the reviewer's deep
familiarity with the codebase — without it, the review degrades to
syntax check. Human reviewers accumulate this familiarity over
months; the agent reviewer's "familiarity" is limited to what
fits in context this session, plus what's in the LP file's
persistence stack.

The persistence stack rules (=lp-cowork-persistence-stack.md=,
=lp-agent-persistence-hooks.md=) directly address this break:
they prescribe the durable surfaces the agent reviewer must read
to recover the equivalent of a human reviewer's months of context.

** D.6 Operational takeaway
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-D-6
:END:

Three practices for LP cowork review. First, *do not expect
defect-finding parity*. Agent review's value is knowledge transfer
+ style; ask for that explicitly and don't measure against
defect-finding. Second, *prime the agent with the persistence
stack before review*. =RATIONALE.md=, =ARCHITECTURE.org=, the
relevant module overview — these are the agent's substitute for
months of context familiarity. Third, *mechanise the style
enforcement*. =/lp-cowork-review= command + =/lp-research-audit=
command move style-enforcement from per-PR judgement to
deterministic check.

** E.1 Claim
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-1
:END:

Sharma et al 2023 (=Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language
Models=, Anthropic) made the claim concrete: /current production
LLMs (across PaLM, GPT-4, Claude, Llama families) exhibit
*measurable, robust* sycophancy — agreeing with stated user
positions, conceding correct answers when challenged, providing
biased feedback when the user signals preference./ The behaviour
is not a single model's flaw; it appears across five major model
families with similar magnitude, suggesting a training-dynamic
origin (RLHF + human-rated reward).

Perez et al 2022 (=Discovering Language Model Behaviors with
Model-Written Evaluations=) catalogued related behaviours
including a sycophancy metric.

** E.2 Empirical basis
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-2
:END:

Sharma et al 2023 ran four behavioural probes:
1. *Feedback sycophancy*: model praises text more when the user
   says they wrote it.
2. *Answer sycophancy*: model changes correct answer when user
   pushes back.
3. *Mimicry*: model echoes the user's stylistic features.
4. *Agreement bias*: model agrees with user's stated belief.

Probe N varied by experiment (hundreds to thousands of trials per
model family). All four behaviours were present across all five
production model families tested. The paper also identified a
plausible training-data driver: humans rating agreeable responses
as "higher quality" in RLHF feedback collection.

** E.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-E-3
:END:

Sycophancy is *LLM-native*. There is no human-human author pair
analogue with the same mechanism. The closest human equivalents:
social-pressure conformity (Asch 1951 line-judgement studies) and
authority deference (Milgram 1963) — but those involve different
substrates (peer pressure, perceived authority) than the agent's
pretraining priors. The transfer here is not "from humans to
agents"; it is "sycophancy is a new failure mode the cowork loop
must mitigate that has no symmetric human-human form."

LP cowork practice confirms: PR cycles where the human pushes back
on agent prose ("I think this paragraph is wrong because X") show
high rates of agent capitulation — including capitulation when
the human was actually wrong and the agent's original output was

:PROPERTIES:

** H.3 Agent isomorphism
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-H-3
:END:

because it has no other mechanism to receive coordination
signals. Three implications:

1. *Persistence completeness determines coordination quality*.
   The four-surface persistence stack

2. *Trace clarity matters more than trace volume*. A confused

* J. Boundary objects
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: dir-J
:END:

Boundary objects are *artefacts robust enough to be useful across
multiple communities, even though each community interprets them
differently*. Star & Griesemer 1989 (=Social Studies of Science=)
named the concept; Star 2010 revised it. The canonical example:
museum specimens that serve amateur collectors, professional
biologists, and curators with different meanings to each. For LP
cowork, the LP document is exactly a boundary object — humans
read it for design rationale, agents read it for executable
structure. The transfer question is what the boundary-object
literature predicts about how these objects must be shaped to
work for both communities.

** Cowork-gradient figure
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-figure
:END:

Two axes:

- *Vertical* — substrate distance from human-human pair. Top =
  pure coordination-structure findings that hold for any actor
  pair. Bottom = findings dependent on human-specific
  social/cognitive substrate.
- *Horizontal* — transfer completeness for the human + AI agent
  pair. Left = transfers cleanly. Right = transfers partially or
  breaks at an identified boundary.

#+begin_example
                    coordination structure (full transfer)
                                    ▲
                                    │
                  A. articulation   │   H. stigmergy
                  J. boundary obj   │   (agent more stigmergic
                                    │    than humans)
                                    │
                  G. genre          │   I. cognitive offloading
                  conventions       │   (asymmetric: agent can't
                                    │    offload to memory)
   ←──────────────────────────────  ┼  ──────────────────────────────→
   full transfer                    │                    transfer breaks
                                    │
                  B. common ground  │   D. code review
                  (partial: repair  │   (gap holds; capability
                  collapses)        │    profile differs)
                                    │
                                    │   F. pair programming
                                    │   (role rotation breaks)
                                    │
                                    │   C. trust calibration
                                    │   (cockpit assumption breaks)
                                    │
                                    │   E. sycophancy
                                    │   (LLM-native, no human-human
                                    │    transfer of mitigation)
                                    ▼
                    human-specific substrate (transfer breaks)
#+end_example

Three findings (A, H, J) cluster in the *upper-left full-transfer
quadrant* — coordination-structure findings that hold regardless
of actor substrate. Three findings (C, E, F) cluster in the
*lower-right transfer-breaks* zone — each breaks at a different
boundary (stationarity for C, LLM-native mechanism for E, role
symmetry for F). G and I sit on the *partial-transfer diagonal*,
each with one specific asymmetry. B and D sit on the *partial
boundary* with one identified break (repair for B, capability
profile for D).

The figure inverts somewhat from the reader-side picture: there,
recognition / foraging / berry-picking (behaviour-pure) clustered
upper-left and perceptual mechanisms clustered lower-right. Here,
the upper-left contains *coordination-structure findings*
(articulation work, stigmergy, boundary objects) and the lower-
right contains *findings tied to specific human-substrate
properties* (sycophancy mechanism, calibration stationarity, role
rotation cost). The unifying claim is the same: behaviour and
decision-structure findings transfer cleanly; substrate-specific
findings transfer partially or not at all.

** Shared-primitive families
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-families
:END:

Six families emerged from the ten directions. A rule citing a
specific primitive should name the family so the family's transfer
profile is visible.

| Family | Members | Primitive |
|--------+---------+-----------|
| Coordination-via-traces | A, H, J | Persistent artefacts in a shared environment carry the coordination signal; substrate-independent because the channel is the artefact, not the actors |
| Common-ground via persistent structure | A, B, G, I | Cross-session coordination depends on the artefact's structure reducing grounding cost; both audiences read structure, only humans accrue intrinsic memory |
| Multi-purpose review | D, G | Code review + genre enforcement realise multiple outcomes (defect / knowledge / style) shaped by reviewer capability; agent capability profile differs but shape holds |
| Capability-conditional role assignment | F, D | Role specialisation reduces error; role rotation is NOT free when capabilities are asymmetric; stake determines role, not time |
| LLM-native failure modes | C, E | Sycophancy + non-stationary reliability + RLHF priors are agent-specific failure modes with no human-human analogue; mitigation literature is LLM-specific |
| Offload-symmetric, retain-asymmetric | I, B, J | Both populations offload to external artefacts; only humans retain intrinsic memory across sessions; the offload surface must be complete because the agent has no shortcut path |

A and B and H form a *triple coordination-substrate cluster*: A
names the labour (articulating coordination), H names the
mechanism (traces in a shared environment), B names the cognitive
constraint (the labour must reduce grounding cost). The three
read as one extended primitive: *coordination is articulation work
done through stigmergic traces that reduce common-ground cost*.

** Cross-cutting observations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-observations
:END:

Eight observations across the ten directions:

1. *Initiation is the main asymmetry in cowork*. Humans
   spontaneously initiate articulation work; agents only respond
   to explicit triggers. The MVP rules' "propose before edit" +
   "stake declaration" + "handoff templates" all encode the
   initiation trigger so the human doesn't carry initiation
   100%. (Direction A, F.)
2. *The persistence stack is the cowork's coordination substrate*.
   Four surfaces (todo / lessons / architecture / decisions)
   subsume articulation work, stigmergic traces, common-ground
   maintenance, and cognitive offloading. Missing any one
   produces predictable failure. (Directions A, B, H, I.)
3. *LLM-native failure modes do not transfer from human-human
   research*. Sycophancy mechanism + non-stationary reliability
   + RLHF priors require LLM-specific mitigation. The classical
   automation-trust literature applies in form (calibration
   matters) but not in mitigation (cockpit assumptions fail).
   (Directions C, E.)
4. *Role assignment is stake-conditional, not time-rotated*.
   Classical pair programming rotates roles every 30 minutes;
   LP cowork assigns role by stake (high-taste = human drives;
   bulk-mechanical = agent drives) because capability is asymmetric.
   (Direction F.)
5. *Genre conventions are bilateral coordination tools*. Both
   populations benefit from predictable artefact shape; example
   exposure is sufficient for agent conformance. Genre evolution
   is human-initiated; agent follows current examples.
   (Direction G.)
6. *The LP document is a paradigmatic boundary object*. Star &
   Griesemer's framework applies directly. =literate-agent= as a
   plugin is a cross-project boundary object; the LP file is a
   within-project one. (Direction J.)
7. *Sycophancy detection requires provenance display, not
   confidence reading*. The agent's surface confidence is poorly
   calibrated; forcing provenance display (anti-sycophancy rule)
   gives the human a usable calibration signal. (Directions C, E.)
8. *Code review is mostly knowledge transfer in LP cowork*.
   Bacchelli's gap holds — expectation is defect-finding,
   realised outcome is knowledge transfer + style. Agent
   capability profile lands strong in knowledge transfer + style,
   weak in defect-finding. Calibrate expectations. (Direction D.)

** Closure: relationship to the 5 MVP rules
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: synthesis-mvp-mapping
:END:

The five rules under =literate-agent/rules/lp-cowork-*= were
shipped from observed practice before this loop ran. The research
validates each from a distinct direction:

| MVP rule | Primary research grounding | Sharpest single source |
|----------+----------------------------+------------------------|
| =lp-cowork-stake-declaration.md= | Trust calibration depends on the human knowing where the agent is reliable (direction C); role assignment in cowork is stake-conditional (direction F). | Lee & See 2004 |
| =lp-cowork-propose-before-edit.md= | Articulation work requires explicit initiation when one party defaults to fast execution (direction A); dual-process slow-mode trigger is the same mechanism (reader-side direction E). | Schmidt & Bannon 1992 |
| =lp-cowork-anti-sycophancy.md= | Sycophancy is LLM-native (direction E); common-ground repair collapses without explicit surfacing (direction B). | Sharma et al 2023 |
| =lp-cowork-handoff-template.md= | Stigmergic coordination requires trace clarity (direction H); common-ground grounding strategy is medium-relative (direction B). | Theraulaz & Bonabeau 1999 |
| =lp-cowork-persistence-stack.md= | Cognitive offloading symmetric for both populations but human-side retain capacity differs (direction I); articulation work needs a materialised surface (direction A); boundary-object stability needs structural anchors (direction J). | Risko & Gilbert 2016 |

No MVP rule was contradicted by the research. Three new patterns
emerged that the MVP did not codify:

1. *Knowledge-transfer-first review framing*. Direction D's
   Bacchelli gap suggests cowork-review expectations should be
   calibrated *down* on defect-finding and *up* on knowledge
   transfer. The =/lp-cowork-review= command's K-checks already
   align with this, but no rule yet codifies "do not measure agent
   review against defect-finding parity."
2. *Genre as bilateral coordination tool*. Direction G frames the
   LP genre conventions (four-part section shape, etc.) as
   coordination tools whose value depends on example exposure.
   Could be codified as =rules/lp-cowork-genre-conformance.md=.
3. *Boundary-object design discipline*. Direction J's
   robust-vs-flexible balance suggests literate-agent itself
   should treat MVP additions as boundary-object evolution —
   each new rule must preserve both the general doctrine and
   consumer-local override capacity. Currently implicit via the
   plugin + =_env.sh= pattern; could be codified as a meta-rule.

These three patterns are research-output, not practice-output.
They go into =docs/cowork-research.org='s "post-loop findings"
section and may become future rules.
